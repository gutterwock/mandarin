# Numbers 0–100

## [phase:atomic] Zero to Ten
tags: numbers, basic-counting, pronunciation

The foundation of Chinese numbers is the sequence from zero to ten. Each digit has a unique character and pronunciation:

- 零 (líng) — zero
- 一 (yī) — one
- 二 (èr) — two
- 三 (sān) — three
- 四 (sì) — four
- 五 (wǔ) — five
- 六 (liù) — six
- 七 (qī) — seven
- 八 (bā) — eight
- 九 (jiǔ) — nine
- 十 (shí) — ten

These ten characters form the building blocks for all numbers in Chinese. Unlike some languages that have separate words for numbers like "eleven" or "twenty," Chinese constructs larger numbers by combining these basic elements logically.

## [phase:atomic] Numbers Eleven Through Nineteen
tags: numbers, teens, construction-pattern

Chinese constructs the numbers 11–19 by combining 十 (shí, "ten") with the single digits. The pattern is logical: "ten-one" for 11, "ten-two" for 12, and so forth:

- 十一 (shí yī) — eleven (literally "ten-one")
- 十二 (shí èr) — twelve (literally "ten-two")
- 十三 (shí sān) — thirteen
- 十四 (shí sì) — fourteen
- 十五 (shí wǔ) — fifteen
- 十六 (shí liù) — sixteen
- 十七 (shí qī) — seventeen
- 十八 (shí bā) — eighteen
- 十九 (shí jiǔ) — nineteen

This transparent construction makes learning teens straightforward once you know 1–10.

## [phase:atomic] Multiples of Ten (20–90)
tags: numbers, tens, construction-pattern

For multiples of ten from 20 to 90, Chinese places the multiplier before 十. The pattern is: digit + 十 = that many tens:

- 二十 (èr shí) — twenty (literally "two-ten")
- 三十 (sān shí) — thirty (literally "three-ten")
- 四十 (sì shí) — forty
- 五十 (wǔ shí) — fifty
- 六十 (liù shí) — sixty
- 七十 (qī shí) — seventy
- 八十 (bā shí) — eighty
- 九十 (jiǔ shí) — ninety

This consistent pattern makes it easy to form any multiple of ten once you know the basic digits.

## [phase:atomic] Compound Numbers (21–99)
tags: numbers, compound-numbers, construction-pattern

Numbers from 21 to 99 combine the patterns you've learned. The structure is: (digit + 十) + digit. For example:

- 二十一 (èr shí yī) — twenty-one (literally "two-ten-one")
- 三十五 (sān shí wǔ) — thirty-five (literally "three-ten-five")
- 四十七 (sì shí qī) — forty-seven
- 五十九 (wǔ shí jiǔ) — fifty-nine
- 八十三 (bā shí sān) — eighty-three
- 九十九 (jiǔ shí jiǔ) — ninety-nine

Every number follows this logical construction. There are no irregular forms to memorize.

## [phase:atomic] One Hundred
tags: numbers, hundred, construction-pattern

The number 100 is expressed as:

- 一百 (yī bǎi) — one hundred

The character 百 (bǎi) means "hundred." In everyday speech, 一百 is the standard form for 100; 一 is generally not omitted.

For numbers beyond 100, the pattern continues logically: 一百零一 (yī bǎi líng yī) for 101, where 零 (líng) explicitly marks the absence of tens.

## [phase:atomic] The Distinction Between 二 and 两
tags: numbers, liang-vs-er, grammar

Mandarin has two words for "two": 二 (èr) and 两 (liǎng). Understanding when to use each is essential:

**二 (èr)** is used:
- In counting sequences: 一, 二, 三...
- In compound numbers: 十二 (12), 二十 (20), 三十二 (32)
- When stating the digit itself

**两 (liǎng)** is used:
- Before measure words: 两个人 (liǎng gè rén, "two people"), 两本书 (liǎng běn shū, "two books")
- For the number 200: 两百 (liǎng bǎi)
- In some fixed expressions

When simply counting or forming numbers like 12, 22, or 92, always use 二. When quantifying nouns with measure words, use 两.

## [phase:atomic] Reading Prices with 块
tags: numbers, prices, measure-words

In everyday Chinese, prices are commonly expressed using 块 (kuài), the informal word for yuan (the Chinese currency unit). The structure is: number + 块:

- 五块 (wǔ kuài) — five yuan
- 十块 (shí kuài) — ten yuan
- 二十块 (èr shí kuài) — twenty yuan
- 五十块 (wǔ shí kuài) — fifty yuan

When the price involves "two" as the main number, use 两: 两块 (liǎng kuài) means "two yuan." However, in compound numbers like 12 or 22, use 二: 十二块 (shí èr kuài), 二十二块 (èr shí èr kuài).

## [phase:atomic] Writing Quantities
tags: numbers, quantities, measure-words

When expressing quantities in Chinese, numbers are combined with measure words. The structure is: number + measure word + noun. For the number two in this context, always use 两 (liǎng):

- 三个 (sān gè) — three (of general items)
- 五本 (wǔ běn) — five (books/volumes)
- 两个 (liǎng gè) — two (of general items)
- 八只 (bā zhī) — eight (animals/certain objects)

Even though the measure word differs based on what's being counted, the number construction remains consistent with the patterns you've learned.

## [phase:atomic] Arabic Numerals in Chinese Context
tags: numbers, arabic-numerals, mixed-writing

Modern Chinese writing frequently mixes Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3...) with hanzi, especially in:

- Prices: ¥15, 20块
- Dates: 5月, 2023年
- Addresses: 3号楼
- Lists and data: ordered items, statistics

When reading these aloud, convert the Arabic numerals to their Chinese equivalents. For example, "15块" is read as 十五块 (shí wǔ kuài). Both writing systems are widely used and understood, with Arabic numerals particularly common in financial and technical contexts.

## [phase:complex] Counting Patterns and Consistency
tags: numbers, patterns, system-logic

Chinese number construction follows an elegant, consistent pattern across all ranges. Understanding this system allows you to generate any number logically:

**Pattern summary:**
- Single digits (1–9): unique characters
- Teens (11–19): 十 + digit
- Tens (20, 30...90): digit + 十
- Compound (21–99): digit + 十 + digit
- Hundred: 一百

This regularity means that once you master 0–10 and understand the construction rules, you can express any number up to 100 without memorizing individual cases. The logic extends beyond 100 as well, with similar patterns for thousands and ten-thousands.

Contrast this with English, where "eleven" and "twelve" are irregular, and "thirteen" through "nineteen" have a different structure than "twenty-one" through "ninety-nine."

## [phase:complex] Using 二 and 两 in Context
tags: numbers, liang-vs-er, usage-contexts

The choice between 二 (èr) and 两 (liǎng) depends on grammatical context, not meaning. Both mean "two," but their usage follows specific rules:

**Use 二 for:**
- Pure counting: 一, 二, 三
- Compound numbers: 十二 (12), 二十 (20), 三十二 (32), 九十二 (92)
- Telephone numbers and room numbers: 二零二房间
- Mathematical operations: 二加三

**Use 两 for:**
- Before measure words: 两个苹果 (two apples), 两本书 (two books)
- The number 200: 两百 is the standard modern Mandarin form for 200; 二百 appears in some regional dialects and classical usage but is not standard
- Telling time: 两点 (two o'clock)
- Ages with measure words: 两岁 (two years old)

**Mixed contexts:**
- 二十两 would mean "twenty-two taels" (两 as a measure of weight)
- 两千二 means "2,200" (both uses of "two" appear)

The distinction is grammatical, not semantic. Native speakers learn this through exposure, but the rule is consistent once understood.

## [phase:complex] Prices and Commercial Numbers
tags: numbers, prices, commerce, practical-usage

In Chinese commerce, numbers appear in several contexts with specific conventions:

**Price expressions:**
- Informal: number + 块 (kuài) — e.g., 五十块
- Formal: number + 元 (yuán) — e.g., 五十元
- With cents: 五块五 or 五块五十 (5.5 or 5.50 yuan)

**Quantity expressions:**
When buying items: number + measure word — e.g., 三个 (three items), 两斤 (two jin/catties)

**Discounts and offers:**
- 八折 (bā zhé) — 20% off (literally "eight-tenths")
- 五十块钱 (wǔ shí kuài qián) — adds 钱 (money) for emphasis

**Mixed notation:**
Modern receipts and signs often mix: "¥38" or "38元" or "38块"

For amounts with "two," remember: 两块 (two yuan), but 十二块 (twelve yuan), 二十块 (twenty yuan).

## [phase:complex] Reading Compound Numbers Fluently
tags: numbers, reading, fluency

Reading longer numbers requires breaking them into their component parts. Practice by identifying the place values:

**Two-digit numbers:**
- 67: 六十七 (six-ten-seven)
- 89: 八十九 (eight-ten-nine)
- 43: 四十三 (four-ten-three)

**Numbers with two:**
- 22: 二十二 (two-ten-two) — note both use 二
- 72: 七十二 (seven-ten-two)
- 20: 二十 (two-ten)

**Reading strategy:**
1. Identify the tens digit (multiply by 10 in Chinese: digit + 十)
2. Add the ones digit
3. Apply special rules (两 before measure words, prices, etc.)

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Saying 两十 instead of 二十 for "twenty"
- Reversing digits: 十五 vs. 五十
- Forgetting 十 in compound numbers
