# Telling the Time

## [phase:atomic] Basic Time Question Structure
tags: time, questions, grammar

To ask "What time is it?" in Mandarin, use **几点了？** (jǐ diǎn le?), literally "how many o'clock?"

The question word **几** (jǐ) means "how many" or "what number," **点** (diǎn) means "o'clock" or "hour," and **了** (le) indicates a current state.

A more complete form adds **现在** (xiànzài, "now"): **现在几点？** (xiànzài jǐ diǎn?) — "What time is it now?"

Both forms are common and interchangeable for asking the current time.

## [phase:atomic] Telling Hours with 点
tags: time, hours, numbers

To tell the time in hours, use the pattern: **[number] + 点** (diǎn).

Examples:
- 一点 (yī diǎn) — 1 o'clock
- 三点 (sān diǎn) — 3 o'clock
- 五点 (wǔ diǎn) — 5 o'clock
- 十二点 (shí'èr diǎn) — 12 o'clock

Simply place the number (1-12) before 点 to indicate the hour. This forms the foundation of all time expressions in Mandarin.

## [phase:atomic] Adding Minutes with 分
tags: time, minutes, numbers

To express minutes, add **分** (fēn) after the number of minutes: **[hour]点[minutes]分**.

Examples:
- 三点十分 (sān diǎn shí fēn) — 3:10
- 五点二十分 (wǔ diǎn èrshí fēn) — 5:20
- 七点五分 (qī diǎn wǔ fēn) — 7:05

The character **分** (fēn) means "minute." Always state the hour first, then the minutes.

## [phase:atomic] Expressing Half Past with 半
tags: time, half-past, special-expressions

To say "half past" an hour, use **半** (bàn) after the hour: **[hour]点半**.

Examples:
- 两点半 (liǎng diǎn bàn) — 2:30
- 九点半 (jiǔ diǎn bàn) — 9:30
- 十一点半 (shíyī diǎn bàn) — 11:30

**半** (bàn) means "half" and represents 30 minutes. Note: Use **两** (liǎng) for "2" when telling time, not 二 (èr).

## [phase:atomic] Morning Period: 上午
tags: time, time-periods, morning

**上午** (shàngwǔ) means "morning" or "AM," specifically referring to the time from early morning until noon.

Place 上午 before the time: **上午[hour]点**.

Examples:
- 上午八点 (shàngwǔ bā diǎn) — 8:00 AM
- 上午十点半 (shàngwǔ shí diǎn bàn) — 10:30 AM

上午 helps clarify whether you're referring to morning hours.

## [phase:atomic] Noon: 中午
tags: time, time-periods, noon

**中午** (zhōngwǔ) means "noon" or "midday," generally referring to the time around 12:00 PM and early afternoon (roughly 11:00 AM to 1:00 PM).

Examples:

- 中午 (zhōngwǔ) — at noon/lunchtime

中午 is commonly used when discussing lunch time.

## [phase:atomic] Using 两 for "2 o'clock"
tags: time, numbers, special-cases

When telling time, use **两** (liǎng) instead of 二 (èr) for the number 2 in hours.

Correct: **两点** (liǎng diǎn) — 2 o'clock
Incorrect: ~~二点~~

This is the same **两** used in 两个 (two of something). However, for minutes, you can use 二: 十二分 (12 minutes) is acceptable.

Examples:
- 两点 (liǎng diǎn) — 2:00
- 两点半 (liǎng diǎn bàn) — 2:30
- 十二点 (shí'èr diǎn) — 12:00 (uses 二 because it's part of 十二)

## [phase:complex] Constructing Complete Time Expressions
tags: time, time-periods, complete-expressions

Complete time expressions combine time period + hour + minutes:
**[time period] + [hour]点 + [minutes]分/半**

Examples:
- 上午九点十五分 (shàngwǔ jiǔ diǎn shíwǔ fēn) — 9:15 AM
- 下午三点半 (xiàwǔ sān diǎn bàn) — 3:30 PM
- 晚上八点四十分 (wǎnshang bā diǎn sìshí fēn) — 8:40 PM

The time period always comes first, providing context for the numerical time. You can omit the time period if context makes it clear, but including it eliminates ambiguity.

## [phase:complex] Distinguishing Time Periods
tags: time, time-periods, context

Choosing the correct time period word depends on the actual time of day:
- **上午** (shàngwǔ): morning, sunrise to noon (~6 AM - 12 PM)
- **中午** (zhōngwǔ): noon/lunchtime (~11 AM - 1 PM)
- **下午** (xiàwǔ): afternoon (~1 PM - 6 PM)
- **晚上** (wǎnshang): evening/night (~6 PM onwards)

There is some overlap between periods. For instance, 12:30 could be 中午十二点半 or 下午十二点半 depending on context. Generally, 中午 is reserved for times very close to 12:00, while 下午 is used for clear afternoon hours.

晚上 begins around dinner time (6-7 PM) and continues through the night.

## [phase:complex] Responding to Time Questions
tags: time, questions, responses, daily-routines

When asked 你几点...? respond with a complete sentence: **我[time period][hour]点([minutes]分/半)[verb]**.

Question: 你几点起床？(What time do you get up?)
Answer: 我上午七点起床。(I get up at 7:00 AM.)

Question: 你几点睡觉？(What time do you go to sleep?)
Answer: 我晚上十一点半睡觉。(I go to sleep at 11:30 PM.)

The time expression comes before the verb. If the time period is obvious from context, you may omit it: 我七点起床 (I get up at 7:00).

## [phase:complex] Reading Analog and Digital Time
tags: time, practical-application

When reading time displays:
- **Digital**: 14:30 → 下午两点半 or 下午两点三十分
- **Analog**: Clock showing 3:15 → 三点十五分 or 上午/下午三点十五分
- **12-hour format**: Use appropriate time period (上午/下午/晚上)
- **24-hour format**: Hours 13-24 use 下午 (13-18) or 晚上 (19-24); subtract 12 from the hour number: 19:00 → 晚上七点

Remember: Mandarin typically uses 12-hour time in spoken contexts, with time periods clarifying AM/PM.

## [phase:complex] Omitting 分 in Time Expressions
tags: time, minutes, spoken-style

In casual speech, **分** (fēn) can be omitted when stating minutes, though it's always correct to include it.

With 分: 三点十五分 (sān diǎn shíwǔ fēn)
Without 分: 三点十五 (sān diǎn shíwǔ)

Both mean "3:15" and are widely used. However:
- **半** (bàn) is never omitted: always say 三点半, never ~~三点三十~~
- In formal contexts or written time, including 分 is more common
- For single-digit minutes, 分 is more commonly retained: 五点五分 is more natural than 五点五

*Okay, see you at 3:30 PM!*

Which statements are true? (Select all that apply)
a: 小明八点上课
b: 小红七点十五分起床
c: 小明和小红中午十二点吃午饭
d: 小红晚上十一点半睡觉
answer: abd
explanation: Statement C is incorrect because it asserts that both 小明 and 小红 eat lunch at noon (中午十二点). However, 小明 eats at 12:00 (中午十二点) while 小红 eats at 12:30 (中午十二点半), so they do not eat at the same time.
