## [phase:atomic] Sentence-final 了 marks completion or change of state
tags: grammar, aspect-marker, sentence-final

The particle 了 (le) placed at the end of a sentence indicates that an action has been completed or that a new situation has emerged. This is one of the most common uses of 了 in Mandarin.

**Structure:** Subject + Verb + 了

Note: This basic pattern works with intransitive verbs (verbs with no objects). When objects are present, the structure becomes Subject + Verb + Object + 了 (see "Position of sentence-final 了" section below).

Examples:
- 我吃了。(Wǒ chī le.) — I ate. / I've eaten.
- 他来了。(Tā lái le.) — He came. / He's here.
- 她走了。(Tā zǒu le.) — She left. / She's gone.

The sentence-final 了 signals that the action is complete or that a change has occurred. In "他来了", it doesn't just mean "he came" but often conveys "he has arrived" or "he's here now."

## [phase:atomic] Position of sentence-final 了
tags: grammar, word-order, sentence-structure

Sentence-final 了 always appears at the very end of the sentence, after the verb and any objects or complements.

**Pattern:** Subject + Verb + (Object) + 了

Examples:
- 我吃饭了。(Wǒ chī fàn le.) — I ate. / I've eaten.
- 他看书了。(Tā kàn shū le.) — He read. / He's been reading.
- 她喝水了。(Tā hē shuǐ le.) — She drank water. / She's had water.

Notice that 了 comes after everything else in the sentence, marking the entire statement as complete or representing a new situation.

## [phase:atomic] Verb-suffix 了 vs. sentence-final 了
tags: grammar, aspect-marker, comparison

Mandarin has two positions for 了, and they serve different functions:

**Verb-suffix 了:** Placed immediately after the verb, before the object. It emphasizes the completion of that specific action.
- 我吃了饭。(Wǒ chī le fàn.) — I ate rice. / I've eaten rice.

**Sentence-final 了:** Placed at the end of the sentence. It indicates a completed action or a change of state affecting the whole sentence.
- 我吃饭了。(Wǒ chī fàn le.) — I've eaten. / I ate (and that's the current situation).

Sometimes both appear together:
- 我吃了饭了。(Wǒ chī le fàn le.) — I've eaten (emphatic completion).

## [phase:atomic] Sentence-final 了 with intransitive verbs
tags: grammar, verb-types, sentence-patterns

With intransitive verbs (verbs that don't take objects), sentence-final 了 directly follows the verb.

**Pattern:** Subject + Intransitive Verb + 了

Common examples:
- 他来了。(Tā lái le.) — He came. / He's arrived.
- 她走了。(Tā zǒu le.) — She left. / She's gone.
- 你睡了。(Nǐ shuì le.) — You slept. / You went to sleep.
- 我去了。(Wǒ qù le.) — I went. / I left.

These sentences often carry a sense of "now" — the new state resulting from the completed action.

## [phase:atomic] Negating completed actions with 没有
tags: grammar, negation, aspect-marker

To negate a completed action, Chinese uses 没(有) (méi (yǒu)), NOT 不. The 了 disappears in negative sentences.

**Affirmative:** 我吃了。(Wǒ chī le.) — I ate.
**Negative:** 我没吃。(Wǒ méi chī.) or 我没有吃。(Wǒ méiyǒu chī.) — I didn't eat.

**Affirmative:** 他来了。(Tā lái le.) — He came.
**Negative:** 他没来。(Tā méi lái.) or 他没有来。(Tā méiyǒu lái.) — He didn't come.

Key rule: 没(有) + Verb (no 了)

## [phase:atomic] 没有 with objects
tags: grammar, negation, sentence-structure

When negating sentences with objects, 没有 comes before the verb, and 了 is removed.

**Affirmative:** 我吃了饭。(Wǒ chī le fàn.) — I ate rice.
**Negative:** 我没有吃饭。(Wǒ méiyǒu chī fàn.) — I didn't eat rice. / I haven't eaten.

**Affirmative:** 她看了书。(Tā kàn le shū.) — She read books.
**Negative:** 她没有看书。(Tā méiyǒu kàn shū.) — She didn't read books.

The full form 没有 is more common with objects, though 没 alone is also acceptable.

## [phase:atomic] Change of state meaning
tags: grammar, aspect-marker, semantics

Sentence-final 了 often emphasizes a change of state — something is now different from before.

Examples of change of state:
- 我饿了。(Wǒ è le.) — I'm hungry (now). / I've become hungry.
- 天冷了。(Tiān lěng le.) — It's cold (now). / It got cold.
- 她累了。(Tā lèi le.) — She's tired (now). / She got tired.

This usage highlights that a new situation has emerged, contrasting with a previous state.

## [phase:atomic] Questions with 了
tags: grammar, question-formation, sentence-patterns

Yes-no questions about completed actions use 吗 (ma) at the end, and 了 remains in the sentence.

**Statement:** 你吃了。(Nǐ chī le.) — You ate.
**Question:** 你吃了吗?(Nǐ chī le ma?) — Did you eat?

**Statement:** 他来了。(Tā lái le.) — He came.
**Question:** 他来了吗?(Tā lái le ma?) — Did he come?

The pattern is: Subject + Verb + 了 + 吗?

## [phase:atomic] Common errors with 了
tags: grammar, error-correction, learning-tips

Common mistakes learners make with 了:

**Error 1:** Using 不 to negate completed actions
- ❌ 我不吃了 (wrong meaning: I'm not going to eat anymore)
- ✓ 我没吃 (I didn't eat)

**Error 2:** Using 了 for habitual or general actions
- ❌ 我每天吃了饭 (incorrect)
- ✓ 我每天吃饭 (I eat every day)

**Error 3:** Keeping 了 in negative sentences
- ❌ 我没有吃了
- ✓ 我没有吃

Remember: 了 marks completion or change, not just past time.

## [phase:complex] Distinguishing verb-suffix and sentence-final 了 in context
tags: grammar, aspect-marker, context

Understanding when to use verb-suffix 了, sentence-final 了, or both depends on what you want to emphasize.

**Verb-suffix 了 only:** Emphasizes completion of a specific action, often continuing with more information.
- 我吃了饭，然后去了学校。(Wǒ chī le fàn, ránhòu qù le xuéxiào.) — I ate, then went to school.

**Sentence-final 了 only:** Indicates a completed action or new state as a standalone fact.
- 我吃饭了。(Wǒ chī fàn le.) — I've eaten. (responding to "Have you eaten?")

**Both together:** Emphasizes both completion and the resulting state.
- 我吃了饭了。(Wǒ chī le fàn le.) — I've finished eating. (very emphatic)

## [phase:complex] Responding to questions with 了
tags: communication, dialogue, pragmatics

In conversations, 了 plays a crucial role in natural responses.

**Question:** 你吃饭了吗?(Nǐ chī fàn le ma?) — Have you eaten?
**Affirmative:** 吃了。(Chī le.) or 我吃了。(Wǒ chī le.) — Yes, I have.
**Negative:** 没吃。(Méi chī.) or 我没有吃。(Wǒ méiyǒu chī.) — No, I haven't.

**Question:** 他来了吗?(Tā lái le ma?) — Has he arrived?
**Affirmative:** 来了。(Lái le.) — Yes, he has.
**Negative:** 没来。(Méi lái.) — No, he hasn't.

Notice how the affirmative keeps 了 but the negative uses 没(有) without 了.

## [phase:complex] Time words and 了
tags: grammar, time-expressions, aspect-marker

了 can coexist with time words, but the meaning differs from English past tense.

**With specific time:** The time word sets the timeframe, 了 marks completion.
- 我昨天吃了饭。(Wǒ zuótiān chī le fàn.) — I ate yesterday.
- 他今天来了。(Tā jīntiān lái le.) — He came today.

**Without time word:** 了 alone indicates recent completion or relevance to now.
- 我吃了饭。(Wǒ chī le fàn.) — I've eaten. (recently, relevant now)

**Important:** Even without 了, past time words can indicate past actions:
- 我昨天吃饭。(Wǒ zuótiān chī fàn.) — I ate yesterday. (了 optional here)

## [phase:complex] Completion vs. duration
tags: grammar, aspect-marker, contrast

了 marks completion, but Chinese uses different structures for ongoing actions or duration.

**Completion (了):**
- 我学了中文。(Wǒ xué le Zhōngwén.) — I studied Chinese. (completed action)

**Duration (no 了, uses time expression):**
- 我学了三年中文。(Wǒ xué le sān nián Zhōngwén.) — I studied Chinese for three years. (verb-suffix 了 with duration)
- 我学中文学了三年。(Wǒ xué Zhōngwén xué le sān nián.) — I've studied Chinese for three years.

**Ongoing state (no 了, or with duration):**
- 我在学中文。(Wǒ zài xué Zhōngwén.) — I'm studying Chinese. (ongoing now)

Sentence-final 了 emphasizes completion, while duration structures focus on the time span.

## [phase:complex] Change of state with adjectives
tags: grammar, adjectives, state-change

Sentence-final 了 with adjectives indicates a change to that state.

**Structure:** Subject + Adjective + 了

Examples showing transitions:
- 天黑了。(Tiān hēi le.) — It got dark. / It's dark now.
- 水热了。(Shuǐ rè le.) — The water got hot. / The water is hot now.
- 我好了。(Wǒ hǎo le.) — I'm better now. / I recovered.
- 便宜了。(Piányí le.) — It became cheaper. / It's cheaper now.

Without 了, adjectives describe current or general states without emphasizing change:
- 天黑。(Tiān hēi.) — It's dark. (general observation)

## [phase:complex] Negative questions and responses
tags: grammar, negation, question-formation

Negative questions with 没(有) check if something hasn't happened.

**Question:** 你没吃饭吗?(Nǐ méi chī fàn ma?) — Haven't you eaten? / Didn't you eat?

**Responses:**
- If you DID eat: 吃了。(Chī le.) — (Yes,) I ate.
- If you DIDN'T eat: 没吃。(Méi chī.) or 对，我没吃。(Duì, wǒ méi chī.) — Right, I didn't eat.

Chinese answers focus on confirming or denying the action itself, not the negative structure.

## [phase:complex] Modal verbs and 了
tags: grammar, modal-verbs, sentence-structure

Modal verbs (想 xiǎng, 要 yào, 会 huì, 能 néng, 可以 kěyǐ) interact with 了 in specific ways.

**Completed intention or realized ability:**
- 我想吃了。(Wǒ xiǎng chī le.) — I feel like eating now. (change: now I want to eat)
- 他会说了。(Tā huì shuō le.) — He can speak now. (change: he learned)

**Completed action after modal:**
- 我要吃了饭。(Wǒ yào chī le fàn.) — I ate as intended. (less common structure)

**Future intention (no 了):**
- 我想吃饭。(Wǒ xiǎng chī fàn.) — I want to eat.

The 了 shows either change of state with the modal or completion of the intended action.
