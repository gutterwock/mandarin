# Activity Verbs

## [phase:atomic] 吃 (chī) — to eat
tags: verb, eating, basic-action

The verb **吃** (chī) means "to eat." It's one of the most essential daily activity verbs in Mandarin.

**Pronunciation:** chī (first tone, high and level)

**Common usage:**
- 吃饭 (chī fàn) — to eat (a meal) / eat rice
- 吃面 (chī miàn) — to eat noodles
- 吃鱼 (chī yú) — to eat fish

**Basic sentence pattern:**
Subject + 吃 + Object

Examples:
- 我吃饭。(Wǒ chī fàn.) — I eat.
- 他吃面。(Tā chī miàn.) — He eats noodles.

## [phase:complex] Verb-Object Combinations
tags: collocation, sentence-structure

Many Mandarin verbs naturally pair with specific objects to form common expressions. Learning these **verb-object collocations** helps you sound natural.

**Common fixed combinations:**
- 吃饭 (chī fàn) — eat rice/meal (not just "eat rice" but "eat a meal")
- 喝水 (hē shuǐ) — drink water
- 看书 (kàn shū) — read books
- 听音乐 (tīng yīnyuè) — listen to music
- 说话 (shuō huà) — speak/talk
- 写字 (xiě zì) — write characters
- 开车 (kāi chē) — drive a car
- 做饭 (zuò fàn) — cook

**Why collocations matter:**
In Mandarin, certain verbs and objects "go together" naturally. For example, when you want to say "eat," you typically say 吃饭 rather than just 吃, even if you're not eating rice specifically.

## [phase:complex] Distinguishing Similar Verbs
tags: distinction, reading-verbs, speaking-verbs

Some HSK 1 verbs have overlapping meanings and require careful distinction.

**看 (kàn) vs. 读 (dú):**
- **看书** — to read silently, using your eyes
- **读书** — to read aloud, using your voice, or to study formally

Both can translate as "read," but 看 emphasizes visual reading, while 读 emphasizes vocalization or studying.

**说 (shuō) vs. 听 (tīng):**
- **说** — to speak (you produce sound)
- **听** — to listen (you receive sound)

These are complementary actions in communication.

**做 (zuò) vs. 工作 (gōng zuò):**
- **做** — general "to do" or "to make" (做饭 = cook)
- **工作** — specifically "to work" (at a job)

## [phase:complex] Building Simple SVO Sentences
tags: sentence-structure, word-order

Mandarin follows a **Subject-Verb-Object (SVO)** word order, similar to English.

**Basic pattern:**
Subject + Verb + Object

**Examples with different subjects:**
- 我看书。(Wǒ kàn shū.) — **I** read books.
- 你喝水。(Nǐ hē shuǐ.) — **You** drink water.
- 他吃饭。(Tā chī fàn.) — **He** eats.
- 她学习中文。(Tā xué xí Zhōngwén.) — **She** studies Chinese.
- 我们工作。(Wǒmen gōng zuò.) — **We** work.

**Key points:**
- The verb always comes after the subject
- The object (if any) comes after the verb
- No conjugation needed — the verb stays the same regardless of subject

## [phase:complex] Two-Character Compound Verbs
tags: compound-verbs, structure

Some HSK 1 verbs are **two-character compounds** that function as single units.

**Two-character verb compounds:**
- **睡觉** (shuì jiào) — to sleep
- **工作** (gōng zuò) — to work
- **学习** (xué xí) — to study

**Structure notes:**
- These compounds must be kept together
- Unlike verb-object combinations (吃饭, 看书), you cannot insert other words between the characters
- They function as single verbs in sentences

**Comparison:**
- 吃饭 = verb (吃) + object (饭) → can be modified: 吃中国饭
- 睡觉 = compound verb → cannot separate: ~~睡中国觉~~

## [phase:integration] Creating Action Sequences
tags: sequential-actions, narrative

Combine multiple activity verbs to create logical action sequences.

**Natural sequences:**
Some activities naturally follow others:
- 买 → 吃 (buy → eat)
- 看书 → 写 (read → write, in a study context)
- 工作 → 睡觉 (work → sleep, end of day)

**Unnatural sequences:**

### question singleChoice difficulty:3
tags: phase:integration, logical-sequence
Which activity sequence is ILLOGICAL?
a: 学习中文 → 写汉字 (study Chinese → write characters)
b: 买书 → 看书 (buy book → read book)
c: 睡觉 → 开车 → 工作 (sleep → drive → work)
d: 做饭 → 吃饭 (cook → eat)
answer: c
explanation: The sequence "sleep → drive → work" is illogical and dangerous. You cannot safely drive immediately after waking up from sleep. All other sequences follow a logical order: studying leads to writing, buying leads to reading, and cooking leads to eating.
