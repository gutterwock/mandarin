## [phase:atomic] V-not-V Question Structure
tags: grammar, question-formation

The A-not-A question pattern (正反问句 zhèngfǎn wènjù) is formed by stating a verb followed by its negation. This creates a yes-no question that presents both options directly.

Basic structure: Subject + Verb + 不 + Verb + (Object)?

Examples:
- 你去不去？(Nǐ qù bù qù?) — Are you going (or not)?
- 他来不来？(Tā lái bù lái?) — Is he coming (or not)?
- 你吃不吃？(Nǐ chī bù chī?) — Are you eating (or not)?

**Note on 你吃不吃?:** While grammatically correct, this phrasing is pragmatically incomplete in real usage. In natural speech, it typically requires an object to be clear (e.g., 你吃不吃饭？"Do you eat rice?"). Without context or an object, the bare form sounds incomplete.

The question literally presents both the positive and negative options, allowing the listener to choose one.

## [phase:atomic] Answering V-not-V Questions
tags: grammar, question-formation

To answer a V-not-V question, you respond with either the positive verb or the negative form (Verb + 不):

Question: 你去不去？(Nǐ qù bù qù?)
Positive answer: 去。(Qù.) — Yes, I'm going.
Negative answer: 不去。(Bù qù.) — No, I'm not going.

You do NOT answer with 是 (shì) or 不是 (bù shì) unless the verb in the question is 是.

## [phase:atomic] V-not-V with 是
tags: grammar, question-formation

The verb 是 (to be) follows the same A-not-A pattern: 是不是 (shì bù shì).

Structure: Subject + 是不是 + Noun/Adjective?

Examples:
- 你是不是学生？(Nǐ shì bù shì xuésheng?) — Are you a student?
- 他是不是老师？(Tā shì bù shì lǎoshī?) — Is he a teacher?
- 这是不是你的书？(Zhè shì bù shì nǐ de shū?) — Is this your book?

Answer with 是 (yes) or 不是 (no).

## [phase:atomic] V-not-V with Adjectives
tags: grammar, question-formation

Adjective verbs (stative verbs) can also form A-not-A questions. Common examples include:

- 好不好？(Hǎo bù hǎo?) — Is it good? / Is that okay?
- 对不对？(Duì bù duì?) — Is it correct?
- 忙不忙？(Máng bù máng?) — Are you busy?

These adjectives function as verbs in Chinese and follow the same V-not-V pattern.

## [phase:atomic] 有没有 Special Pattern
tags: grammar, question-formation

The verb 有 (yǒu - to have) uses a special negative form: 没有 (méiyǒu). Therefore, its A-not-A question pattern is 有没有 (yǒu méiyǒu), not *有不有.

Structure: Subject + 有没有 + Object?

Examples:
- 你有没有时间？(Nǐ yǒu méiyǒu shíjiān?) — Do you have time?
- 他有没有钱？(Tā yǒu méiyǒu qián?) — Does he have money?
- 你有没有书？(Nǐ yǒu méiyǒu shū?) — Do you have a book?

## [phase:atomic] V-not-V vs 吗 Questions
tags: grammar, question-formation, register

Both A-not-A questions and 吗 (ma) questions form yes-no questions in Mandarin. They are functionally equivalent with no inherent difference in politeness or formality.

Compare:
- 你去吗？(Nǐ qù ma?) — Are you going?
- 你去不去？(Nǐ qù bù qù?) — Are you going?

Both are neutral and appropriate in the same contexts. The choice is often stylistic or based on regional/personal preference.

## [phase:atomic] Word Order in V-not-V Questions
tags: grammar, sentence-structure

In V-not-V questions, the word order remains the same as in statements. Only the verb changes to the V-not-V pattern.

Statement: 你吃中国菜。(Nǐ chī Zhōngguó cài.) — You eat Chinese food.
Question: 你吃不吃中国菜？(Nǐ chī bù chī Zhōngguó cài?) — Do you eat Chinese food?

The object (中国菜) stays after the verb phrase.

## [phase:atomic] Abbreviated V-not-V Forms
tags: grammar, spoken-chinese

In rapid or casual speech, the second verb in the A-not-A pattern is sometimes shortened, especially with two-syllable verbs:

Full form: 你喜欢不喜欢？(Nǐ xǐhuān bù xǐhuān?)
Shortened: 你喜不喜欢？(Nǐ xǐ bù xǐhuān?)

The first syllable of the verb is used before 不, but the full verb appears after 不. This is common in spoken Mandarin.

## [phase:atomic] Common V-not-V Question Patterns
tags: vocabulary, patterns

Several V-not-V patterns appear frequently in daily conversation:

- 是不是 (shì bù shì) — is it or not
- 有没有 (yǒu méiyǒu) — have or not
- 好不好 (hǎo bù hǎo) — good or not / okay or not
- 对不对 (duì bù duì) — correct or not
- 要不要 (yào bù yào) — want or not
- 会不会 (huì bù huì) — can or not / know how to or not

These function as common conversational phrases.

## [phase:complex] Choosing Between 吗 and V-not-V
tags: grammar, pragmatics, stylistic-choice

While 吗 questions and V-not-V questions are functionally equivalent, there are subtle contextual preferences:

V-not-V questions can sound slightly more direct or emphatic because they explicitly present both options. They're common when:
- Seeking clarification or confirmation
- The speaker genuinely doesn't know which option applies
- In rapid conversational exchanges

吗 questions are slightly more neutral and often preferred when:
- Asking more tentative or exploratory questions
- First-time inquiries
- The question is longer or more complex

However, these are tendencies, not rules. Both forms work in virtually all contexts.

## [phase:complex] V-not-V with Modal Verbs
tags: grammar, modal-verbs

Modal verbs (能, 会, 要, 想, etc.) form A-not-A questions following the same pattern:

- 你会不会说中文？(Nǐ huì bù huì shuō Zhōngwén?) — Can you speak Chinese?
- 你想不想去？(Nǐ xiǎng bù xiǎng qù?) — Do you want to go?
- 你能不能来？(Nǐ néng bù néng lái?) — Can you come?
- 他要不要喝茶？(Tā yào bù yào hē chá?) — Does he want to drink tea?

The main verb (说, 去, 来, 喝) follows the modal verb and remains unchanged.

## [phase:complex] V-not-V with Objects Between Pattern
tags: grammar, word-order

When the A-not-A pattern uses abbreviated forms or when emphasis is needed, objects can sometimes appear between the verb elements, though the standard form keeps objects after the complete pattern.

Standard: 你看不看这本书？(Nǐ kàn bù kàn zhè běn shū?)
Alternative: 你看这本书不看？(Nǐ kàn zhè běn shū bù kàn?)

The alternative form is theoretically possible but strongly dispreferred in natural speech. Native speakers rarely use this construction—it sounds awkward and non-standard. The standard form is correct and should be used in practice.

## [phase:complex] Combining V-not-V with Time and Place
tags: grammar, sentence-structure

V-not-V questions can include time and place expressions. Following standard Chinese word order, time comes before the verb, and place typically comes before or after the verb depending on the verb type:

- 你明天去不去？(Nǐ míngtiān qù bù qù?) — Are you going tomorrow?
- 你在家吃不吃饭？(Nǐ zài jiā chī bù chī fàn?) — Do you eat at home?
- 他今天来不来学校？(Tā jīntiān lái bù lái xuéxiào?) — Is he coming to school today?

Time expressions: before the verb
Place expressions: 在 + place typically before the verb; destination places after verbs of motion

## [phase:complex] Negative Assumption Questions
tags: pragmatics, discourse

While V-not-V questions are grammatically neutral, context and tone can suggest an expected answer. However, the form itself doesn't encode assumption the way "Don't you...?" does in English.

Compare:
- 你不是学生吗？(Nǐ bù shì xuésheng ma?) — Aren't you a student? [suggests speaker thought you were]
- 你是不是学生？(Nǐ shì bù shì xuésheng?) — Are you a student? [neutral]

The V-not-V form remains neutral regardless of context, while negative + 吗 structures can encode assumptions.

## [phase:complex] Responding to V-not-V Questions with Elaboration
tags: pragmatics, conversation

While short answers (verb alone or negative form) are sufficient, fuller responses are natural in conversation:

Question: 你明天去不去图书馆？
Short: 去。or 不去。
Full: 去，我要看书。(Yes, I need to study.)
Full: 不去，我很忙。(No, I'm busy.)

The verb or its negation can be followed by explanations, making responses more natural and informative.
