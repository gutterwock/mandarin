## [phase:atomic] 我 (wǒ) — I, me
tags: phase:atomic, pronoun, vocabulary

The character **我** (wǒ) means "I" or "me" in Mandarin Chinese. It is one of the most fundamental pronouns you will use.

**Pronunciation:** wǒ (third tone — low-dipping tone: starts mid-low, dips to low, then rises slightly (in isolation); in speech often a low tone)

**Usage:** 我 is used as the subject pronoun when referring to yourself.

Example:
- 我很好。(Wǒ hěn hǎo.) — I am very well.

## [phase:complex] Difference Between 叫 and 是
tags: phase:complex, grammar, verbs

Both **叫** (jiào) and **是** (shì) can be used in introductions, but they serve different purposes:

**叫 (jiào)** = "to be called/named"
- Used specifically for names
- Pattern: 我叫 + name
- Example: 我叫李明。(My name is Li Ming.)

**是 (shì)** = "to be"
- Used for identity, nationality, profession
- Pattern: 我是 + noun/noun phrase
- Example: 我是学生。(I am a student.)
- Example: 我是中国人。(I am Chinese.)

**Key difference:** 
- **我叫X** is the standard pattern for introductions — introducing your name for the first time
- **我是X** is for confirming identity already established in context — stating who you are after your name is known
Both are grammatically correct but serve different communicative functions.

## [phase:complex] Question Word Placement
tags: phase:complex, grammar, word-order

In Mandarin Chinese, question words like **什么** (what) and **哪** (which) stay in the position where the answer would appear. They do not move to the beginning of the sentence like in English.

**Pattern:** Question words replace the information being asked about

**Example with 什么:**
- Statement: 我叫大卫。(I am called David.)
- Question: 你叫什么名字？(You are called what name?)

**Example with 哪:**
- Statement: 我是美国人。(I am America person.)
- Question: 你是哪国人？(You are which-country person?)

This is different from English where "what" and "which" typically move to the beginning: "What is your name?" / "Which country are you from?"

## [phase:integration] Cultural Context of Chinese Introductions
tags: phase:integration, culture, pragmatics

Chinese introductions often differ from Western customs in several ways:

**Information sharing:** Chinese speakers typically share more personal information early in introductions, including hometown, work unit (单位 dānwèi), or school. This helps establish social context and potential connections.

**Family names:** Chinese names put the family name first. 李明 (Lǐ Míng) has the family name 李 and given name 明. When using 我叫, you give your full name.

**Professional titles:** In formal settings, people may introduce themselves with professional titles: 我是李老师 (I am Teacher Li) rather than just 我叫李明.

**Modesty:** Chinese culture values modesty. Even when saying 认识你很高兴, the tone is warm but not overly enthusiastic.

## [phase:integration] Common Mistakes in Introductions
tags: phase:integration, error-analysis, grammar

Learners often make several common mistakes when introducing themselves in Chinese:

**Mistake 1: Wrong pattern for introducing your name**
- ❌ 我是大卫 (when first introducing yourself)
- ✅ 我叫大卫 (standard for introductions)
- ℹ️ Note: Both are grammatically correct but serve different functions — 我叫 introduces your name; 我是 confirms identity already established

**Mistake 2: Wrong word order in questions**
- ❌ 什么你叫名字？
- ✅ 你叫什么名字？

**Mistake 3: Forgetting 人 after country name**
- ❌ 我是美国
- ✅ 我是美国人

**Mistake 4: Using 叫 for nationality**
- ❌ 我叫中国人
- ✅ 我是中国人

**Mistake 5: Wrong question word**
- ❌ 你是什么国人？
- ✅ 你是哪国人？

## [phase:integration] Expanding the Conversation
tags: phase:integration, conversation, application

After basic introductions, conversations naturally expand. Here are common follow-up patterns:

**Asking follow-up questions:**
- 你也是学生吗？(Nǐ yě shì xuésheng ma?) — Are you also a student?
- 你是老师吗？(Nǐ shì lǎoshī ma?) — Are you a teacher?

**Responding with 也 (also):**
- 我也是学生。(Wǒ yě shì xuésheng.) — I am also a student.
- 我也是美国人。(Wǒ yě shì Měiguó rén.) — I am also American.

**Adding information:**
- 我是中国人。我是北京人。(I am Chinese. I am from Beijing.)
- 我叫李明。我是学生。我也是中国人。(My name is Li Ming. I am a student. I am also Chinese.)

**Vocabulary note:**
- 也 (yě) — also, too
- 吗 (ma) — question particle (makes statements into yes/no questions)
