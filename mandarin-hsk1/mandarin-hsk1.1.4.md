# Pinyin: Spelling Rules and Special Cases

## [phase:atomic] Zero-initial i becomes yi
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, zero-initial

When the vowel **i** begins a syllable with no consonant (a "zero initial"), it is written as **yi** in pinyin. This is a spelling convention that makes syllables easier to recognize in written text.

Examples:
- **yi** (one) — pronounced like "ee"
- **yīfu** (衣服, clothes) — the i sound at the start becomes yi

The pronunciation doesn't change; only the spelling does. The letter **y** is not pronounced as a separate sound — it's simply a marker that helps separate syllables.

## [phase:atomic] Zero-initial u becomes wu
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, zero-initial

When the vowel **u** begins a syllable with no consonant, it is written as **wu** in pinyin. Like the yi rule, this is purely a spelling convention.

Examples:
- **wǔ** (five) — pronounced like "oo" in "food"
- **wū yā** (crow) — the u sound at the start of a syllable

The **w** is not pronounced separately; it's a visual marker. The sound remains the pure **u** vowel.

## [phase:atomic] Zero-initial ü becomes yu
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, zero-initial, u-umlaut

When the vowel **ü** begins a syllable with no consonant, it is written as **yu** in pinyin. Notice that the umlaut (two dots) is dropped when written this way.

Examples:
- **yú** (fish) — pronounced with rounded lips, like French "u" in "tu"
- **yǔ** (rain)

The **ü** sound is distinct from **u**, but when spelled as **yu**, the dots disappear. Context tells you it's the ü sound, not the u sound.

## [phase:atomic] Summary of zero-initial spelling rules
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, zero-initial

All three zero-initial vowels follow the same pattern: add a letter marker when the vowel starts a syllable with no consonant.

| Original vowel | Spelled as | Example |
|---|---|---|
| i | yi | yī (one) |
| u | wu | wǔ (five) |
| ü | yu | yǔ (rain) |

These rules apply **only** when the vowel is at the beginning. If a consonant comes before the vowel (like **li**, **lu**, **lü**), the normal spelling is used.

## [phase:atomic] Tone mark placement: the a priority
tags: pinyin, tone-marks, placement-rules

When a syllable contains multiple vowels, the tone mark must be placed on the correct vowel. The vowel **a** always receives the tone mark when present in a syllable.

Examples:
- **hǎo** (good) — mark goes on a, not o
- **mǎi** (buy) — mark goes on a, not i
- **bài** (defeat) — mark goes on a

The rule is simple: if you see an **a** in the syllable, that's where the tone mark goes.

## [phase:atomic] Tone mark placement: when no a is present
tags: pinyin, tone-marks, placement-rules

When a syllable has no **a**, look for **o** or **e** — these vowels receive the tone mark next.

Examples:
- **dōu** (all) — mark goes on o
- **lěng** (cold) — mark goes on e
- **hēi** (black) — mark goes on e

If the syllable has **o** or **e**, the tone mark goes there. These vowels represent the main sound of the syllable.

## [phase:atomic] Tone mark placement: i and u together
tags: pinyin, tone-marks, placement-rules

When **i** and **u** appear together with no a, o, or e present, the tone mark goes on the **second** vowel (whichever comes later in the syllable).

Examples:
- **liú** (flow) — mark goes on u (second vowel)
- **jiǔ** (nine) — mark goes on u (second vowel)
- **guī** (return) — mark goes on i (second vowel)

This is the "second vowel wins" rule for i-u combinations. Look at the order in which they appear.

## [phase:atomic] Complete tone mark placement rule
tags: pinyin, tone-marks, placement-rules

The complete priority order for tone mark placement:

1. **a** (highest priority — always gets the mark)
2. **o** or **e** (if no a is present)
3. When only **i** and **u** together: the second vowel gets the mark

This creates a systematic way to place tone marks correctly every time.

Mnemonic: "**A** is the boss; **o** and **e** are managers; **i** and **u** share based on position."

## [phase:atomic] Introduction to erhua (儿化)
tags: pinyin, erhua, pronunciation-features

**Erhua** (儿化) is a phenomenon in Mandarin where the sound "r" is added to the end of a syllable, creating a retroflex effect. In pinyin, this is shown by adding **r** directly after the syllable.

Examples:
- **huār** (flower) — 花儿
- **wánr** (to play) — 玩儿
- **yìdiǎnr** (a little bit) — 一点儿

The **r** is not a separate syllable; it modifies the preceding syllable by curling the tongue during pronunciation. Erhua is very common in Beijing-area Mandarin.

## [phase:atomic] Erhua pronunciation effect
tags: pinyin, erhua, pronunciation-features

When erhua is applied, the tongue curls back during the pronunciation of the final sound, creating a distinct "r-colored" vowel. The original syllable and the "r" blend into one sound.

Pronunciation changes:
- The final vowel may be modified or dropped
- The tongue retroflex position happens simultaneously with the syllable
- The tone remains on the original syllable, not on the "r"

Example: **huār** sounds like "hwar" with the tongue curled, not "hua" + "er" as two syllables.

## [phase:atomic] Regional variation in erhua
tags: pinyin, erhua, dialectal-variation

Erhua usage varies significantly by region in China. Beijing and northern dialects use erhua frequently, while southern dialects (like Cantonese-influenced Mandarin) use it rarely or not at all.

- **Northern Mandarin**: Heavy erhua usage (huār, nǎr, wánr)
- **Standard Mandarin (Putonghua)**: Moderate erhua usage
- **Southern Mandarin**: Minimal or no erhua

In HSK exams and formal learning, erhua appears in standard pinyin transcription, but learners from different regions may pronounce it differently in natural speech.

## [phase:complex] Applying zero-initial rules in context
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, zero-initial

When writing pinyin for complete words, you must apply zero-initial spelling rules consistently. Consider the word for "one one two two" (chaotic): **yī yī èr èr**.

Notice:
- The syllables with "i" at the start become **yī**, not *ī
- The number "two" (èr) keeps its consonant, so no rule applies
- Each syllable is treated independently

When you encounter syllables starting with i, u, or ü sounds without consonants, immediately think: yi, wu, or yu.

## [phase:complex] Tone mark placement in multi-vowel syllables
tags: pinyin, tone-marks, placement-rules

Real-world syllables often require careful analysis of the vowel combination to place tone marks correctly. Consider these examples:

- **xiǎo** (small): contains **i**, **a**, **o** → mark goes on **a** (highest priority)
- **jiǔ** (nine): contains **i**, **u** → mark goes on **u** (second vowel)

Always scan for **a** first, then **o/e**, then apply the second-vowel rule for **i-u** combinations.

## [phase:complex] Distinguishing ü-related spellings
tags: pinyin, u-umlaut, spelling-rules

The ü vowel appears in multiple spelling contexts, and distinguishing them is important:

1. **After j, q, x**: always written **u** (no dots): **ju**, **qu**, **xu**
2. **Zero initial**: written **yu** (no dots): **yú** (fish)
3. **After n, l**: written **ü** (with dots): **nǚ** (female), **lǜ** (green)

The key insight: j, q, x can **never** be followed by the regular u sound, so when you see "ju", you know it must be the ü sound. The dots are unnecessary.

## [phase:complex] Erhua in common expressions

## [phase:integration] Writing a complete sentence with spelling rules
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, comprehensive

Consider the sentence: "Five fish are here" → **Wǔ tiáo yú zài zhèr.**

Analysis:
- **Wǔ**: zero-initial u → wu, tone on u
- **tiáo**: contains i, a, o → tone mark on a
- **yú**: zero-initial ü → yu (no dots), tone on u
- **zài**: contains a, i → tone mark on a
- **zhèr**: erhua on location word

Every syllable requires conscious application of the rules. This integration is what fluent pinyin writing demands.

## [phase:integration] Correcting pinyin spelling errors
tags: pinyin, spelling-rules, error-correction

Real learners make predictable errors with spelling rules. Developing the ability to spot and correct these errors solidifies your understanding.

Common errors:
- Writing **i** instead of **yi** (forgetting zero-initial rule)
- Placing tone mark on wrong vowel: *máo* instead of **mǎo**
- Confusing **u** and **ü**: writing **yǔ** instead of **yù** for "jade"
- Treating erhua as a separate syllable: writing **huā ér** instead of **huār**
