## [phase:atomic] 你好 (nǐ hǎo) – Hello
tags: greeting, basic, informal

你好 (nǐ hǎo) is the most common greeting in Mandarin Chinese, equivalent to "hello" in English. It's used throughout the day in both casual and semi-formal situations.

**Pronunciation:** nǐ hǎo (knee how)
- 你 (nǐ) = you
- 好 (hǎo) = good/well

This greeting is appropriate when meeting friends, classmates, colleagues, or people of similar age and social status. You can use it at any time of day.

## [phase:atomic] 您好 (nín hǎo) – Formal Hello
tags: greeting, formal, respect

您好 (nín hǎo) is the formal version of 你好. It shows respect and is used when addressing people of higher status, older people, or in professional settings.

**Pronunciation:** nín hǎo (neen how)
- 您 (nín) = you (formal/respectful)
- 好 (hǎo) = good/well

Use 您好 when greeting:
- Teachers or professors
- Elderly people
- Business clients
- Strangers in formal settings
- Anyone you want to show extra respect to

The key difference is just one character: 您 (formal you) replaces 你 (informal you).

## [phase:atomic] 早上好 (zǎoshang hǎo) – Good Morning
tags: greeting, time-specific, morning

早上好 (zǎoshang hǎo) is used specifically in the morning to greet someone. It's the equivalent of "good morning" in English.

**Pronunciation:** zǎo-shang hǎo (dzow-shahng how)
- 早上 (zǎoshang) = morning/early
- 好 (hǎo) = good

This greeting is used from sunrise until approximately 11 a.m. or noon. It's friendly and appropriate in both casual and formal settings.

A shortened informal version, 早 (zǎo), is also commonly used among friends and family.

## [phase:atomic] 晚上好 (wǎnshang hǎo) – Good Evening
tags: greeting, time-specific, evening

晚上好 (wǎnshang hǎo) is used to greet someone in the evening. It's equivalent to "good evening" in English.

This greeting is typically used from late afternoon (around 6 p.m.) onwards when you're greeting someone, but before it's time to sleep. It works in both casual and formal contexts.

Note the similar structure to 早上好 – both follow the pattern [time of day] + 好.

## [phase:atomic] 再见 (zàijiàn) – Goodbye
tags: farewell, departure, basic

再见 (zàijiàn) is the standard way to say "goodbye" in Mandarin Chinese. It's used when parting from someone.

**Pronunciation:** zài-jiàn (dzai-jyen)
- 再 (zài) = again
- 见 (jiàn) = see/meet

Literally, 再见 means "see again," similar to how "see you later" works in English. It's appropriate in most situations, from casual to formal.

Unlike greetings that change based on time of day, 再见 can be used any time you're saying goodbye.

## [phase:atomic] 晚安 (wǎn'ān) – Good Night
tags: farewell, night, sleep

晚安 (wǎn'ān) is used specifically when parting from someone at night before going to sleep. It's equivalent to "good night" in English.

**Pronunciation:** wǎn-ān (wahn-ahn)
- 晚 (wǎn) = evening/late
- 安 (ān) = peaceful/safe

Unlike 晚上好 (good evening), which is a greeting, 晚安 is a farewell used when someone is going to bed or sleep. You wouldn't use 晚安 when arriving somewhere in the evening – only when leaving or ending communication for the night.

## [phase:atomic] Responding to 你好
tags: response, conversation, basic

When someone greets you with 你好, the most common response is to say 你好 back. This creates a simple, natural exchange.

**Basic exchange:**
- Person A: 你好！(Hello!)
- Person B: 你好！(Hello!)

You can also respond with 您好 if you want to show extra respect to the person greeting you, even if they used 你好 first. This is common when addressing someone older or in a position of authority.

The response mirrors the greeting – it's straightforward and friendly.

## [phase:complex] Formality Levels in Greetings
tags: formality, social-hierarchy, context

Mandarin Chinese has built-in formality distinctions that reflect social relationships. Understanding when to use 你 (nǐ) versus 您 (nín) is crucial for appropriate communication.

**Informal (你好):**
- Friends and peers
- Family members (younger or same generation)
- Classmates
- People younger than you

**Formal (您好):**
- Elders and seniors
- Teachers and professors
- Business associates and clients
- Service encounters in formal settings
- Anyone you're meeting for the first time in a professional context

When in doubt with strangers, starting with 您好 is safer – you can always become less formal later. Going from informal to formal is more awkward.

The choice isn't just about politeness; it reflects Chinese cultural values of respect for age, authority, and social position.

## [phase:complex] Time-Based Greeting Selection
tags: time-appropriateness, context, decision-making

Choosing the right greeting in Mandarin depends on the time of day and context. While 你好/您好 works anytime, time-specific greetings show cultural awareness.

**Time-based decision tree:**
- **Morning (sunrise to ~11 a.m.):** 早上好 or 早 (informal)
- **Midday to late afternoon:** 你好/您好 (time-specific greetings less common)
- **Evening (~6 p.m. onward, when greeting):** 晚上好
- **Night (when parting for sleep):** 晚安

**Key distinction:** 晚上好 = arriving/greeting in evening; 晚安 = departing/going to sleep

If you're unsure of the time or context, 你好/您好 is always safe and never wrong. Time-specific greetings are an enhancement, not a requirement.

## [phase:complex] Cultural Context: Greetings in Chinese Society
tags: culture, social-norms, pragmatics

Chinese greetings reflect deeper cultural values beyond mere translation. Understanding context prevents cultural missteps.

**Key cultural points:**

1. **Frequency:** Chinese people don't greet every time they pass someone they know (unlike some Western cultures). Greeting is mainly for the first encounter of the day or when initiating an interaction.

2. **Formality matters:** The 你/您 distinction reflects hierarchical respect. Using 你 with someone who expects 您 can seem disrespectful.

3. **Group settings:** When entering a room with multiple people, you might say 你好 or 大家好 (hello everyone) generally, rather than greeting each person individually.

4. **Business context:** Greetings are often accompanied by a handshake or slight bow, especially when using 您好.

5. **Digital communication:** In texts or online, people often use 你好 regardless of formality, though 您 still appears in very formal written communication.

## [phase:integration] Error Analysis and Correction
tags: common-mistakes, troubleshooting, refinement

Understanding common errors helps you refine your greeting usage and sound more natural.

**Common mistakes:**

1. **Using 晚安 as a greeting:** 晚安 is only for saying goodnight before sleep, not for greeting someone in the evening.
   - ❌ Wrong: *(arriving at 8 p.m.)* 晚安！
   - ✓ Correct: *(arriving at 8 p.m.)* 晚上好！or 你好！

2. **Wrong formality level:** Using 你好 when 您好 is expected.
   - ❌ Wrong: *(to elderly stranger)* 你好！
   - ✓ Correct: *(to elderly stranger)* 您好！

3. **Time mismatch:** Using morning greetings in afternoon.
   - ❌ Wrong: *(at 3 p.m.)* 早上好！
   - ✓ Correct: *(at 3 p.m.)* 你好！or 您好！

4. **Overusing time-specific greetings:** While correct, 你好/您好 is often more practical.
   - Acceptable but less common: *(at 2 p.m.)* Always using 你好
   - Also correct: Using 你好/您好 at any time

**Recovery tip:** If you use the wrong greeting, Chinese speakers are usually understanding with learners. Simply correct yourself or continue naturally—communication matters more than perfection.

## [phase:integration] Cultural Immersion: Reading Social Cues
tags: pragmatics, social-awareness, advanced-application

Advanced usage involves reading situational cues to select optimal greetings, not just grammatically correct ones.

**Situational awareness factors:**

1. **First encounter:** More formal initially (您好 safer), can relax to 你好 as relationship develops.

2. **Group dynamics:** If others are using 您好 with someone, follow suit.

3. **Professional settings:** Default to 您好 unless explicitly told otherwise.

4. **Regional variation:** Some regions use greetings more frequently than others; observe and adapt.

5. **Age ambiguity:** If someone looks close to your age but you're unsure, 您好 is safer.

6. **Reciprocity:** If someone uses 您好 with you, they're signaling formality—respond accordingly.

**Advanced insight:** Native speakers often judge second-language speakers' cultural competence by greeting appropriateness more than grammar perfection. Choosing the right formality level signals social awareness.
