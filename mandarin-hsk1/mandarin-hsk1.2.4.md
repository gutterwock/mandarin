# How Are You and Simple Responses

## [phase:atomic] The Basic Question 你好吗？
tags: question-form, grammar

The phrase **你好吗？** (nǐ hǎo ma?) literally means "Are you well?" and is used to ask how someone is doing.

**Structure:**
- 你 (nǐ) = you
- 好 (hǎo) = good/well
- 吗 (ma) = question particle

Adding 吗 to the end of a statement turns it into a yes/no question. So 你好 (you're well) becomes 你好吗？ (are you well?).

## [phase:atomic] The Standard Positive Response 我很好
tags: response, grammar

The most common positive response to 你好吗？ is **我很好** (wǒ hěn hǎo) meaning "I'm very well."

**Structure:**
- 我 (wǒ) = I/me
- 很 (hěn) = very
- 好 (hǎo) = good/well

Note: In Chinese, 很 is often used even when you don't mean "very" in English. It's the standard way to form "I am well."

## [phase:atomic] Adding Thank You 谢谢
tags: response, courtesy

After responding 我很好, it's polite to add **谢谢** (xièxie) meaning "thank you."

A complete polite response is: **我很好，谢谢。** (Wǒ hěn hǎo, xièxie.) = "I'm very well, thank you."

The comma in Chinese writing (，) works like a comma in English, separating phrases within a sentence.

## [phase:atomic] Returning the Question 你呢？
tags: question-form, conversation

After answering, you can ask the same question back using **你呢？** (nǐ ne?) meaning "And you?" or "How about you?"

**Structure:**
- 你 (nǐ) = you
- 呢 (ne) = question particle for "and/how about"

This is much shorter than repeating the full question 你好吗？

## [phase:atomic] The Neutral Response 还好
tags: response, vocabulary

**还好** (hái hǎo) means "so-so," "not bad," or "okay." It's a neutral response when things are neither great nor terrible.

**Structure:**
- 还 (hái) = still/yet/fairly
- 好 (hǎo) = good

This is less enthusiastic than 我很好 but not negative.

## [phase:atomic] The Negative Response 不太好
tags: response, grammar

**不太好** (bù tài hǎo) means "not very well" or "not so good."

**Structure:**
- 不 (bù) = not
- 太 (tài) = too/very
- 好 (hǎo) = good

This is a polite way to indicate you're not doing well without being too direct or dramatic.

## [phase:atomic] Expressing Busyness 很忙
tags: response, vocabulary

**很忙** (hěn máng) means "very busy."

**Structure:**
- 很 (hěn) = very

You can respond with 我很忙 (wǒ hěn máng) to mean "I'm very busy" when asked how you are.

## [phase:atomic] Combining Responses 还好，很忙
tags: response, conversation

You can combine responses to give more information. For example: **还好，很忙** (hái hǎo, hěn máng) means "Okay, (but) very busy."

This acknowledges things are fine overall while mentioning you're busy. Chinese allows these compound responses naturally.

## [phase:atomic] Realistic Usage Context
tags: cultural-note, usage

While **你好吗？** is taught in textbooks, native speakers use it less frequently in daily conversation. It can sound somewhat formal or textbook-like.

However, it's perfectly acceptable and understood, especially:
- In formal settings
- When learning the language
- In written communication

At higher levels, phrases like 最近怎么样？ (How have you been recently?) are more common in casual speech.

## [phase:complex] Full Conversation Exchange
tags: conversation, dialogue

A complete exchange using 你好吗？ follows this pattern:

**Person A:** 你好吗？  
**Person B:** 我很好，谢谢。你呢？  
**Person A:** 我也很好。

Note the use of **也** (yě) meaning "also/too" in the response. This is natural when both people are doing well.

**Alternative responses for Person B:**
- 还好，谢谢。
- 不太好。
- 我很忙。

## [phase:complex] Understanding 很 in Different Contexts
tags: grammar, adverb

The character **很** (hěn) technically means "very," but its usage differs from English "very":

**With 好:** 我很好 is the standard way to say "I'm well" — it doesn't emphasize "very" strongly.

**With 忙:** 我很忙 does emphasize being quite busy.

**With other adjectives:** 很 is often required grammatically even when no emphasis is intended.

In Chinese grammar, simple adjectives often need 很 before them when used as predicates.

## [phase:complex] Responding Without Full Sentences
tags: conversation, informal

In casual conversation, Chinese speakers often give short responses:

**Question:** 你好吗？

**Short responses:**

- 还好。
- 很忙。
- 不太好。

These omit 我 (I) because it's understood from context. While learners should practice full sentences (我很好), understanding short forms helps with comprehension.

## [phase:integration] Choosing Appropriate Responses
tags: application, social-context

The response you choose to 你好吗？ depends on:

1. **Relationship:** Close friends vs. formal acquaintances
2. **Context:** Casual meeting vs. professional setting
3. **Your actual state:** Honest vs. polite generic response
4. **Cultural expectations:** Chinese often give modest responses

In professional settings, 还好 or 我很好 are safe. With close friends, you can be more honest (不太好，很累).

## [phase:integration] Constructing Natural Dialogues
tags: application, conversation

Create natural-sounding exchanges by:

2. **Being reciprocal:** If asked 你呢?, respond and ask back

4. **Using appropriate closings:** 加油, 要多休息, etc.

## [phase:integration] Cultural Competence in Greetings
tags: cultural-context, pragmatics

Understanding 你好吗？ in cultural context:

**In textbooks:** Standard greeting exchange
**In reality:** Less common than English "How are you?"
**Native preference:** More specific questions (about recent events, work, family)
**Learning stage:** Perfectly acceptable to use while learning
**Politeness:** Always understood and appreciated, even if textbook-like

As you advance, learn alternatives like:
- 最近怎么样？ (How have you been recently?)
- 吃了吗？ (Have you eaten? — traditional greeting)
- 忙吗？ (Are you busy?)
