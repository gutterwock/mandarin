## [phase:atomic] 谢谢 (xièxie) — Thank you
tags: vocabulary, gratitude, pinyin

**谢谢** (xièxie) means "thank you" in Mandarin Chinese.

- Character: 谢谢
- Pinyin: xièxie
- Tone: both syllables use 4th tone (falling)

This is the most common way to express gratitude in daily conversations. You can use it in any situation where you want to thank someone.

## [phase:atomic] 不客气 (bú kèqi) — You're welcome
tags: vocabulary, response, pinyin

**不客气** (bú kèqi) means "you're welcome" and is the standard response to 谢谢.

- Character: 不客气
- Pinyin: bú kèqi
- Tone pattern: 2nd tone (tone sandhi from 4th) + 4th tone + light/neutral tone
- Literal meaning: "don't be polite"

Use this phrase when someone thanks you to acknowledge their gratitude politely.

## [phase:atomic] Tone distinction — 谢 vs 请
tags: pronunciation, tone, contrast

**谢** (xiè) in 谢谢 and **请** (qǐng) use different tones:

- **谢** (xiè) — 4th tone (falling, emphatic)
- **请** (qǐng) — 3rd tone (dipping)

Getting the tone right is essential for clear communication. A 4th tone falls sharply; a 3rd tone dips down then rises slightly.

## [phase:complex] Gratitude exchange in context
tags: dialogue, pragmatics, social-interaction

A complete gratitude exchange in Mandarin follows this pattern:

**Person B:** 不客气。(You're welcome.) *or* 没关系。(No problem.)

The exchange is brief and polite. In Chinese culture, excessive thanks or elaborate responses are less common than in some Western cultures.

## [phase:complex] Distinguishing 请 from 对不起
tags: contrast, semantics, pragmatics

Though both are polite expressions, **请** and **对不起** serve completely different functions:

- **请 (qǐng):** makes polite requests or invitations; forward-looking action
- **对不起 (duìbuqǐ):** apologizes for past actions; acknowledges a mistake

Using 对不起 when you mean 请 (or vice versa) creates confusion. Remember: 请 invites action, 对不起 acknowledges wrongdoing.

## [phase:complex] Tone pairs in common phrases
tags: pronunciation, tone-combinations, fluency

Several courtesy phrases contain important tone combinations:

- **谢谢** (xièxie): 4th + 4th tone
- **对不起** (duìbuqǐ): 4th + 2nd + 3rd tone
- **不客气** (bú kèqi): 2nd tone (due to tone sandhi) + 4th tone + light/neutral tone (气 is often neutral/light in natural speech)
- **没关系** (méi guānxi): 2nd tone + 1st tone + light/neutral tone

Practicing these tone combinations builds fluency and natural-sounding speech.

## [phase:integration] Cultural context — Courtesy phrase intensity
tags: culture, pragmatics, comparison

In Mandarin Chinese social interactions, courtesy phrases are used somewhat differently than in English:

- Thanks and apologies are expressed, but often more briefly
- Multiple repetitions (like "thank you so much, really, I mean it") are less common
- Responses are typically simple: 不客气, 没关系
- Over-thanking or over-apologizing may seem insincere or create awkwardness

The phrases you've learned represent complete, appropriate expressions in most situations.
