# Simple Dialogue: Shopping and Ordering

## [phase:atomic] 我要 — "I want"
tags: phase:atomic, grammar, requests

The phrase 我要 (wǒ yào) means "I want" and is the most direct way to order or request something in Chinese. It's perfectly acceptable in shops and restaurants.

**Structure:** 我要 + [thing]

Examples:
- 我要这个。(Wǒ yào zhège.) — I want this one.
- 我要水。(Wǒ yào shuǐ.) — I want water.
- 我要两个。(Wǒ yào liǎng ge.) — I want two.

This phrase is more direct than English "I'd like," but it's the standard way to order in Chinese-speaking environments.

## [phase:atomic] Asking price with 多少钱
tags: phase:atomic, questions, shopping

To ask "How much?" in Chinese, use 多少钱? (duōshao qián?). This literally means "how much money?"

**Usage:**
- 这个多少钱? (Zhège duōshao qián?) — How much is this?
- 那个多少钱? (Nàge duōshao qián?) — How much is that?

You can point at items while asking this question. The shopkeeper will respond with a number followed by 块 (kuài) or 元 (yuán) for the currency unit.

## [phase:atomic] Price responses — 块 (kuài)
tags: phase:atomic, money, vocabulary

When someone tells you a price, they'll use 块 (kuài) or 元 (yuán) for yuan (the Chinese currency). 块 is the colloquial, everyday term used in shops and restaurants.

**Examples:**
- 五块。(Wǔ kuài.) — Five yuan.
- 十块。(Shí kuài.) — Ten yuan.
- 三十块。(Sānshí kuài.) — Thirty yuan.

In formal contexts or written text, you'll see 元 (yuán) instead, but in spoken shopping situations, 块 is standard.

## [phase:atomic] 给你 — "Here you are"
tags: phase:atomic, courtesy, transactions

给你 (gěi nǐ) literally means "give you" but functions as "here you are" when handing something to someone. Both customers and shopkeepers use this phrase.

**When to use it:**
- When handing over money: 给你。(Gěi nǐ.)
- When giving items: 给你。(Gěi nǐ.)
- When passing something: 给你水。(Gěi nǐ shuǐ.) — Here's your water.

This is a practical, everyday phrase used constantly in transactions.

## [phase:atomic] 谢谢 — "Thank you"
tags: phase:atomic, courtesy, politeness

谢谢 (xièxie) means "thank you" and is essential in shopping interactions. Both customers and service workers use it throughout a transaction.

**Common usage:**
- After receiving something: 谢谢。(Xièxie.)
- After being told information: 谢谢。(Xièxie.)
- When leaving: 谢谢。(Xièxie.)

You may also hear 谢谢你 (xièxie nǐ), which includes "you" explicitly, but 谢谢 alone is perfectly polite and more common in quick transactions.

## [phase:atomic] 不客气 — "You're welcome"
tags: phase:atomic, courtesy, responses

不客气 (bú kèqi) is the standard response to 谢谢. It means "you're welcome" or literally "don't be polite."

**Usage:**
- Customer: 谢谢。(Xièxie.)
- Shopkeeper: 不客气。(Bú kèqi.)

This phrase closes the courtesy exchange. You might also hear 不用谢 (búyòng xiè) which means "no need to thank," but 不客气 is more common.

## [phase:atomic] Polite particle 请 (qǐng)
tags: phase:atomic, courtesy, grammar

请 (qǐng) means "please" and can make requests more polite. It comes before the verb.

**Structure:** 请 + [verb phrase]

Examples:
- 请给我。(Qǐng gěi wǒ.) — Please give me.
- 请问 (Qǐngwèn) — May I ask...

While 我要 is perfectly acceptable in ordering, adding 请 makes your request more courteous. However, in fast-paced casual restaurants, 我要 alone is standard and expected.

## [phase:atomic] 这个 and 那个 in ordering
tags: phase:atomic, demonstratives, shopping

When ordering, you'll often point and say 这个 (zhège) "this one" or 那个 (nàge) "that one" rather than naming the item.

**In practice:**
- 我要这个。(Wǒ yào zhège.) — I want this one.
- 那个多少钱? (Nàge duōshao qián?) — How much is that one?

This is extremely common in shops and restaurants with visible items. You can combine pointing with these words for clarity.

## [phase:atomic] Numbers in prices — Review
tags: phase:atomic, numbers, money

Understanding numbers 1-100 is crucial for prices. Quick review of key numbers:

**1-10:** 一 (yī), 二 (èr), 三 (sān), 四 (sì), 五 (wǔ), 六 (liù), 七 (qī), 八 (bā), 九 (jiǔ), 十 (shí)

**Teens:** 十 + number (e.g., 十五 shíwǔ = 15)

**Tens:** number + 十 (e.g., 二十 èrshí = 20)

**Compound:** tens + ones (e.g., 二十五 èrshíwǔ = 25)

Listen carefully for these in price responses.

## [phase:atomic] Measure word 个 in ordering
tags: phase:atomic, grammar, measure-words

When ordering quantities, you need the measure word 个 (ge) between the number and the noun.

**Structure:** number + 个 + [noun]

Examples:
- 两个 (liǎng ge) — two (of them)
- 三个苹果 (sān ge píngguǒ) — three apples
- 我要一个。(Wǒ yào yí ge.) — I want one.

Note: Use 两 (liǎng) not 二 (èr) for "two" when counting items. While 二 (èr) is used in compound numbers (二十二 = 22) and for counting abstractly, 两 (liǎng) is specifically used before measure words when counting concrete items.

## [phase:atomic] 还要 — "Also want"
tags: phase:atomic, grammar, ordering

还要 (hái yào) means "also want" and is used to add items to your order.

Alternatively, the shopkeeper might ask "还要吗?" (Hái yào ma?) — "Do you want anything else?"

## [phase:complex] Basic ordering sequence
tags: phase:complex, dialogue, integration

A typical simple ordering sequence follows this pattern:

**1. Order:** 我要[item]. (Wǒ yào [item].)  
**2. Confirm/add:** 还要[item]吗? (Hái yào [item] ma?)  
**3. Ask price:** [Item]多少钱? ([Item] duōshao qián?)  
**4. State price:** [Number]块。([Number] kuài.)  
**5. Payment exchange:** 给你。(Gěi nǐ.)  
**6. Thanks:** 谢谢。(Xièxie.)  
**7. Response:** 不客气。(Bú kèqi.)

Not every step appears in every transaction, but this represents the full flow.

## [phase:complex] Multiple items ordering
tags: phase:complex, dialogue, grammar

When ordering several items, you can list them using 我要 once, or repeat it, or use 还要 for additional items.

**Options:**
- List: 我要这个、那个、一个苹果。(Wǒ yào zhège, nàge, yí ge píngguǒ.)
- Repeat: 我要这个。我要那个。(Wǒ yào zhège. Wǒ yào nàge.)
- Add: 我要这个。还要那个。(Wǒ yào zhège. Hái yào nàge.)

All three are acceptable. The repeat method is clearest for beginners and most common in natural speech.

*Note: The conjunction 和 (hé) means "and" and is used to link items in a list. It appears in option c but may not have been explicitly covered earlier in the course.*

## [phase:complex] Responding to "还要吗?"
tags: phase:complex, dialogue, responses

When a shopkeeper asks "还要吗?" (Hái yào ma? — "Want anything else?"), you have two options:

**If adding more:**  
还要[item]. (Hái yào [item].) — "I also want [item]."

**If finished:**  
不要了,谢谢。(Búyào le, xièxie.) — "No more, thanks."  
OR simply: 不要了。(Búyào le.)

The 了 (le) particle indicates change of state — you wanted things, but now you're done.

## [phase:complex] Price negotiation — accepting the price
tags: phase:complex, dialogue, culture

In most modern shops and restaurants, prices are fixed. When told the price, you typically:

**1. Accept silently** — just hand over money  
**2. Acknowledge:** 好 (hǎo) — "OK"  
**3. Pay:** 给你 (gěi nǐ) — "Here you are"

In some markets, negotiation is possible, but that's beyond HSK 1 scope. For beginners, assume prices are fixed and move straight to payment.

## [phase:complex] Variation: 要 vs 想要
tags: phase:complex, grammar, nuance

Both 要 (yào) and 想要 (xiǎng yào) can express wanting something:

**要 (yào):** Direct, decisive — "want/will have"  
**想要 (xiǎng yào):** Softer, "would like"

In ordering contexts, 我要 is standard and perfectly polite. 想要 is less common in quick transactions but fine to use. Some speakers prefer 想要 as it feels less demanding.

**Both are correct:**
- 我要咖啡。(Wǒ yào kāfēi.)
- 我想要咖啡。(Wǒ xiǎng yào kāfēi.)

## [phase:integration] Handling misunderstanding
tags: phase:integration, dialogue, problem-solving

**When you don't understand:**

Vendor: 这个三十八块。(Zhège sānshíbā kuài.)  
Customer: 对不起,多少? (Duìbuqǐ, duōshao?)  
Vendor: 三十八。三十八块。(Sānshíbā. Sānshíbā kuài.)  
Customer: 好,谢谢。(Hǎo, xièxie.)

**Key phrase:** 对不起,多少? (Duìbuqǐ, duōshao?) — "Sorry, how much?"

You can also say 请再说一次 (qǐng zài shuō yí cì) — "Please say it again." While the individual vocabulary (再 = again, 说 = speak/say) is within HSK 1 scope, the construction as a whole represents a slightly more complex pattern. Simply asking 多少? with a confused expression works perfectly at the HSK 1 level.
