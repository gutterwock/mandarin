## [phase:atomic] 很 (hěn) – Very
tags: degree-adverb, basic-intensifier

The adverb 很 (hěn) means "very" and is the most common degree adverb in Mandarin. It appears before adjectives to indicate intensity.

**Structure:** Subject + 很 + Adjective

- 我很高兴。(Wǒ hěn gāoxìng.) – I am very happy.
- 他很忙。(Tā hěn máng.) – He is very busy.
- 这个很好。(Zhège hěn hǎo.) – This is very good.

**Important note:** In neutral statements with adjectives, 很 is often used even when the meaning is simply "is" rather than "very." For example, "他很好" can mean either "He is very well" or simply "He is well," depending on context and stress.

## [phase:atomic] 太 (tài) – Too/Extremely
tags: degree-adverb, excessive-intensifier

The adverb 太 (tài) means "too" or "extremely" and often carries a connotation of excessiveness. When used with 了 at the end (太...了), it emphasizes an extreme degree.

**Structure:** Subject + 太 + Adjective + (了)

- 太贵了！(Tài guì le!) – Too expensive!
- 这个太难了。(Zhège tài nán le.) – This is too difficult.
- 你太好了！(Nǐ tài hǎo le!) – You're so nice! / You're the best!

**Context matters:** 太 can be negative (indicating a problem) or positive (expressing enthusiasm), depending on the adjective and situation.

## [phase:atomic] 非常 (fēicháng) – Extremely
tags: degree-adverb, neutral-intensifier

The adverb 非常 (fēicháng) means "extremely" or "very" and is more formal than 很. Unlike 太, it is neutral and does not imply that the degree is problematic or excessive.

**Structure:** Subject + 非常 + Adjective

- 我非常喜欢。(Wǒ fēicháng xǐhuan.) – I like it very much.
- 他非常聪明。(Tā fēicháng cōngming.) – He is extremely clever.
- 这非常重要。(Zhè fēicháng zhòngyào.) – This is extremely important.

**Usage note:** 非常 is stronger than 很 and more neutral than 太. It is appropriate in both positive and neutral contexts without suggesting a problem.

## [phase:atomic] 真 (zhēn) – Really
tags: degree-adverb, emphasis

The adverb 真 (zhēn) means "really" or "truly" and adds emphasis to adjectives. It often expresses the speaker's genuine feeling or observation.

**Structure:** Subject + 真 + Adjective

- 真好！(Zhēn hǎo!) – Really good!
- 他真高。(Tā zhēn gāo.) – He is really tall.
- 今天真热。(Jīntiān zhēn rè.) – Today is really hot.

**Tone:** 真 tends to sound more spontaneous and emotional than 很 or 非常, expressing a direct reaction or sincere feeling.

## [phase:atomic] 有点儿 (yǒudiǎnr) – A Bit (Negative Connotation)
tags: degree-adverb, mild-negative

The phrase 有点儿 (yǒudiǎnr) means "a bit" or "a little" but is almost always used with adjectives that describe something undesirable or problematic. It cannot be used with positive adjectives.

**Structure:** Subject + 有点儿 + Adjective (negative/undesirable)

- 我有点儿累。(Wǒ yǒudiǎnr lèi.) – I'm a bit tired.
- 有点儿贵。(Yǒudiǎnr guì.) – A bit expensive.
- 他有点儿不高兴。(Tā yǒudiǎnr bù gāoxìng.) – He's a bit unhappy.

**Important restriction:** Do NOT use 有点儿 with positive adjectives like 好 (good) or 高兴 (happy). You cannot say ❌ 有点儿好.

## [phase:atomic] 比较 (bǐjiào) – Relatively/Rather
tags: degree-adverb, moderate-degree

The adverb 比较 (bǐjiào) means "relatively," "rather," or "comparatively." It indicates a moderate degree, less intense than 很 or 非常, and often implies an implicit comparison or measured assessment.

**Structure:** Subject + 比较 + Adjective

**Usage note:** 比较 suggests a moderate assessment, often softer than 很. It can make statements sound less absolute or more polite.

## [phase:atomic] 不太 (bú tài) – Not Very
tags: degree-adverb, negation

The phrase 不太 (bú tài) means "not very" or "not too" and is used to politely negate or soften the degree of an adjective.

**Structure:** Subject + 不太 + Adjective

- 不太好。(Bú tài hǎo.) – Not very good.
- 我不太累。(Wǒ bú tài lèi.) – I'm not very tired.
- 今天不太冷。(Jīntiān bú tài lěng.) – Today is not very cold.

**Politeness:** 不太 is often more polite than a direct negation with 不. Instead of saying "不好" (not good), saying "不太好" softens the negative assessment.

## [phase:atomic] Placement of Degree Adverbs
tags: word-order, adverb-position

All degree adverbs in this lesson appear directly before the adjective they modify. They follow the subject and any auxiliary verbs.

**Basic pattern:** Subject + Degree Adverb + Adjective

- 我**很**忙。(Wǒ **hěn** máng.)
- 他**非常**高。(Tā **fēicháng** gāo.)
- 这个**有点儿**贵。(Zhège **yǒudiǎnr** guì.)

**With negation:** When using 不, it typically appears before 太 to form 不太, but 很, 非常, 真, and other adverbs generally do not combine with 不 in the same way.

## [phase:complex] Choosing Between 太 and 非常
tags: adverb-selection, connotation

Both 太 and 非常 mean "very" or "extremely," but they differ in connotation. 太 often suggests excessiveness and may imply a problem (though it can be positive with enthusiasm). 非常 is neutral and simply indicates high intensity.

**太 (often problematic):**
- 太贵了！(Too expensive! – I won't buy it)
- 太难了。(Too difficult. – I'm struggling)

**太 (enthusiastic positive):**
- 太好了！(Wonderful! Great!)
- 你太棒了！(You're amazing!)

**非常 (neutral intensity):**
- 非常贵。(Very expensive. – neutral observation)
- 非常难。(Very difficult. – neutral fact)

**Choosing principle:** Use 太...了 when expressing that something exceeds a threshold (positive or negative). Use 非常 for neutral, factual statements of high degree.

## [phase:complex] Using 有点儿 with Appropriate Adjectives
tags: adverb-selection, semantic-restrictions

The restriction on 有点儿 is semantic: it pairs only with adjectives expressing negative, undesirable, or problematic states. This includes physical discomfort, negative emotions, unfavorable conditions, and unwanted qualities.

**Correct uses (undesirable states):**
- 有点儿累 (a bit tired)
- 有点儿冷 (a bit cold)
- 有点儿贵 (a bit expensive)
- 有点儿难 (a bit difficult)
- 有点儿不高兴 (a bit unhappy)

**Incorrect uses (positive/neutral states):**
- ❌ 有点儿好 (cannot say "a bit good")
- ❌ 有点儿高兴 (cannot say "a bit happy")
- ❌ 有点儿便宜 (typically avoid "a bit cheap" – 便宜 is generally positive; however, in contexts where cheapness implies poor quality, 有点儿便宜 may be acceptable)

**Strategy:** Before using 有点儿, ask: "Is this adjective describing something I would complain about or find problematic?" If yes, use 有点儿. If no, choose a different adverb.

## [phase:complex] Politeness and Softening with 比较 and 不太
tags: pragmatics, politeness-strategies

Both 比较 and 不太 serve to soften statements and make them more polite or less absolute. They are particularly useful when giving assessments or making potentially face-threatening statements.

**比较 softens positive and neutral statements:**
- Direct: 我忙。(I'm busy.)
- Softer: 我比较忙。(I'm rather busy – implies but leaves room for flexibility)

**不太 softens negative statements:**
- Direct: 不好。(Not good – blunt)
- Softer: 不太好。(Not very good – less harsh)
- Direct: 我不喜欢。(I don't like it.)
- Softer: 我不太喜欢。(I don't really like it – more polite)

**Social function:** These adverbs help maintain social harmony by avoiding overly strong or absolute statements.

## [phase:complex] Degree Adverbs with 了
tags: particle-usage, sentence-final-le

Some degree adverbs commonly appear with the sentence-final particle 了, particularly 太. The pattern 太...了 emphasizes the excessive or extreme nature of the situation.

**太...了 pattern:**
- 太好了！(Great!)
- 太贵了！(Too expensive!)
- 太冷了！(Too cold!)

**Other adverbs:** While 很, 非常, 真, and 比较 can appear in sentences with 了, they don't form fixed patterns with it the way 太 does. The 了 in such sentences usually marks change of state or new situation, not the adverb itself.

- 真好！(Really good! – common without 了)
- 很好！(Very good! – common without 了)
- 非常好！(Extremely good! – common without 了)

**Usage:** The 太...了 pattern is very common for expressing strong reactions.

## [phase:complex] Stacking and Combining Degree Adverbs
tags: adverb-combination, grammatical-constraints

Generally, degree adverbs do NOT stack or combine with each other in Mandarin. You cannot say ❌ 很非常好 or ❌ 太很贵.

**Single adverb rule:** Use one degree adverb per adjective.

**Exception with negation:** 不太 is a fixed combination where 不 (negation) combines with 太. This is not true stacking since 不 is a negator, not a degree adverb.
- 不太好 ✓ (not very good)
- 不太贵 ✓ (not very expensive)

**Incorrect combinations:**
- ❌ 很太好
- ❌ 非常真好
- ❌ 太比较贵

**Correct approach:** Choose the single adverb that best expresses your intended meaning and intensity.

## [phase:integration] Selecting Appropriate Degree Adverbs by Context
tags: context-analysis, pragmatic-choice

Choosing the right degree adverb requires considering: intensity needed, positive vs. negative connotation, formality level, and social context.

**Context factors:**
1. **Intensity scale:** 有点儿 < 比较 < 很 < 真 < 非常 < 太
   *Note: 有点儿 only applies to negative contexts, making direct comparison with positive adjectives difficult. 真 adds emphasis and sincerity rather than strictly being stronger than 很.*
2. **Connotation:** 有点儿 (negative only), 太 (often excessive), others (neutral/flexible)
3. **Formality:** 非常 (more formal), 真/很 (neutral), 有点儿 (colloquial)
4. **Social appropriateness:** 比较/不太 (softer, more polite)

**Decision process:**
- What degree am I expressing? (mild → extreme)
- Is the adjective positive, negative, or neutral?
- How direct or polite should I be?
- What emotional tone do I want to convey?

## [phase:integration] Correcting Misused Degree Adverbs
tags: error-correction, application

Common errors include: using 有点儿 with positive adjectives, stacking degree adverbs, mismatching formality levels, and choosing adverbs with inappropriate connotations for the context.

**Error patterns:**
1. ❌ 有点儿好 → ✓ 比较好 / 还不错
2. ❌ 很太贵 → ✓ 太贵了 or 很贵
3. ❌ Using 太...了 in formal writing → ✓ 非常
4. ❌ 不很好 → ✓ 不太好
5. ❌ Using 真 in formal contexts → ✓ 非常

**Correction strategy:** Identify the speaker's intended meaning (degree and connotation), then select the adverb that matches both the meaning and the context.

## [phase:integration] Expressing Degrees in Natural Conversations
tags: conversational-application, fluency

Natural conversation requires fluid selection of degree adverbs based on pragmatic goals: agreeing, disagreeing politely, expressing enthusiasm, complaining gently, or making objective observations.

**Conversational strategies:**

*Expressing enthusiasm:*
- 太好了！真好！(Great! Really good!)

*Polite disagreement:*
- 我觉得不太... (I think it's not very...)

*Gentle complaint:*
- 有点儿... (A bit... [problematic])

*Objective observation:*
- 很.../非常... (Very/Extremely...)

*Softening requests or refusals:*
- 我比较... (I'm rather...)

The key is matching the adverb to your communicative goal, not just the literal degree.

### question singleChoice difficulty:4
tags: phase:integration, scenario-based
Your colleague did exceptional work and you want to express sincere praise. Which sounds most genuine and enthusiastic?
a: 你做得非常好。
b: 你做得很好。
c: 你做得太好了！
d: 你做得比较好。
answer: c
explanation: 太好了 expresses genuine enthusiasm and strong positive emotion, perfect for sincere praise. 非常好 is more formal/neutral, 很好 is standard but less enthusiastic, and 比较好 is too moderate for exceptional work.

### question freeText difficulty:4

A student wants to tell their teacher that the homework was difficult, but without sounding like they're complaining too much. What should they say and why?
answer: • Should suggest 作业有点儿难 or 作业比较难 • Explanation: 有点儿难 acknowledges difficulty without excessive complaint; 比较难 is measured and appropriate for teacher-student context • Should avoid 太难了 (sounds like complaining) • Should recognize this requires balancing honesty with appropriate politeness for the context

### question multiChoice difficulty:4
tags: phase:integration, contextual-appropriateness
In which situations would 不太 be more appropriate than direct negation with 不?
a: Politely declining an invitation
b: Giving negative feedback to someone
c: Expressing an absolute prohibition
d: Softening a disagreement
answer: abd
explanation: 不太 is useful for politeness in social situations (A, B, D). For absolute prohibitions (C), direct 不 or 不能 is more appropriate.
