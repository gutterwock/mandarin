## [phase:atomic] HSK 1 Exam Overview
tags: exam-structure, hsk-levels

The HSK (Hanyu Shuiping Kaoshi, 汉语水平考试) is China's standardized test for Mandarin proficiency. HSK 1 is the beginner level, testing basic vocabulary and grammar.

The exam contains **40 questions total** divided into two sections:
- **听力 (tīnglì)** — Listening: 20 questions
- **阅读 (yuèdú)** — Reading: 20 questions

There is no speaking or writing component at HSK 1 level. The entire exam takes approximately 40 minutes, with about 15 minutes for listening and 17 minutes for reading, plus time for filling in answer sheets.

## [phase:atomic] HSK 1 Scoring System
tags: exam-structure, scoring

Each question on the HSK 1 exam is worth **5 points**, giving a maximum of **200 points total**:
- Listening section: 100 points maximum (20 questions × 5 points)
- Reading section: 100 points maximum (20 questions × 5 points)

The passing score is **120 points out of 200**. This means you need to answer at least 24 out of 40 questions correctly to pass. There is no minimum score requirement for each individual section.

## [phase:atomic] Listening Section Structure
tags: listening, exam-structure

The listening section (听力) has 20 questions divided into **four parts**:
- Part 1: Questions 1-5
- Part 2: Questions 6-10
- Part 3: Questions 11-15
- Part 4: Questions 16-20

Each part tests a different skill using different task types. All listening materials are played twice, giving you two chances to hear each audio clip.

## [phase:atomic] Listening Part 1 Format
tags: listening, task-types

**Listening Part 1** (Questions 1-5) tests your ability to match spoken words to pictures. 

For each question, you hear a **single word or short phrase** (played twice). You see **several pictures** and must choose which picture matches what you heard.

This tests basic vocabulary recognition in spoken form, particularly nouns and common objects.

## [phase:atomic] Listening Part 2 Format
tags: listening, task-types

**Listening Part 2** (Questions 6-10) tests comprehension of short sentences through **true/false judgments**.

For each question, you see a **picture** and hear a **sentence** (played twice). You must decide if the sentence correctly describes the picture or not. You mark:
- ✓ (true/correct) if the sentence matches the picture
- ✗ (false/incorrect) if it doesn't match

This tests whether you can understand simple descriptive sentences.

## [phase:atomic] Listening Part 3 Format
tags: listening, task-types

**Listening Part 3** (Questions 11-15) presents **short dialogues** between two people.

For each question, you hear a **brief conversation** (played twice) and then a **question about the conversation**. You must choose the correct answer from several pictures.

This tests your ability to understand context, relationships between speakers, and extract specific information from conversations.

## [phase:complex] Exam Section Comparison
tags: exam-structure, strategy

Understanding the differences between the listening and reading sections helps you prepare effectively:

**Listening section characteristics:**
- Audio played at fixed pace (no control over speed)
- Each item played twice
- Tests spoken vocabulary and natural speech comprehension
- Requires immediate processing and decision-making
- Cannot review previous questions once audio moves forward

**Reading section characteristics:**
- Self-paced (manage your own time)
- Can review and change answers
- Tests character recognition and written vocabulary
- Allows time for careful consideration
- Can use process of elimination more easily

Both sections test similar vocabulary and grammar concepts, but through different modalities. Some students find one section easier than the other based on their learning style and practice focus.

## [phase:complex] Task Type Progression
tags: task-types, exam-structure

The four parts in each section follow a progression from simpler to more complex tasks:

**Listening progression:**
1. Single word → picture (isolated vocabulary)
2. Sentence → picture judgment (sentence comprehension)
3. Dialogue → information question (conversational context)
4. Question → appropriate response (interactive communication)

**Reading progression:**
1. Word → picture (vocabulary recognition)
2. Sentence matching (sentence structure and logic)
3. Fill in the blank (contextual vocabulary use)
4. Sentence → picture or comprehension (full sentence understanding)

Both sections move from recognizing isolated elements to understanding language in context. The difficulty increases as more context and inference are required.

## [phase:complex] Scoring Strategy and Pass Requirements
tags: scoring, strategy

With a passing score of 120/200, you need to answer **24 out of 40 questions correctly** (60% accuracy). This means you can miss up to 16 questions and still pass.

**Strategic implications:**
- There is no penalty for guessing—always answer every question
- You don't need perfection; consistent performance is sufficient
- Missing several questions in one part doesn't mean failure
- Focus on maximizing correct answers overall, not achieving 100% in any section

**Section flexibility:**
Since there's no minimum score per section, you could theoretically score 20/100 on listening and 100/100 on reading (or vice versa) and still pass. However, balanced preparation is recommended since most students perform similarly across both sections.

## [phase:complex] Vocabulary and Task Type Relationships
tags: vocabulary, task-types

The HSK 1 vocabulary of 150 words appears throughout different task types, but certain word categories are more common in specific parts:

**Concrete nouns** (人, 书, 水, etc.): Common in picture-matching tasks (Listening Part 1, Reading Part 1)

**Verbs and adjectives** (看, 大, 好, etc.): Frequently appear in sentence judgments and fill-in-the-blank sections

**Question words** (什么, 谁, 哪儿, etc.): Essential for dialogue comprehension (Listening Parts 3 and 4)

**Numbers, time, and quantity words** (一, 二, 点, 个, etc.): Appear across all sections but especially in dialogues and sentence completion

Understanding which vocabulary types appear in which task formats helps you anticipate what to listen or look for in each part of the exam.

## [phase:integration] Time Management Strategy
tags: strategy, exam-structure

**Exam timing breakdown:**
- Total exam time: approximately 40 minutes
- Listening: ~15 minutes (fixed, controlled by audio)
- Reading: ~17 minutes (flexible, self-paced)
- Answer sheet transfer: ~5-8 minutes

**Strategic time management:**

For listening: You cannot control pace, so focus on staying alert and marking answers immediately. Don't dwell on missed questions—the audio will continue.

For reading: You have approximately 17 minutes for 20 questions, giving you about 50 seconds per question. However, some questions (like picture-word matching) can be answered quickly, giving you more time for fill-in-the-blank questions that require more thought.

**Answer sheet strategy:** Many test centers require you to mark answers on a separate sheet. Some students mark answers in the test booklet first, then transfer later. Others mark directly on the answer sheet. Choose your strategy before exam day.
