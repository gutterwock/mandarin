# Negation: 不 and 没

## [phase:atomic] The basic negator 不 (bù)
tags: negation, bu, basic-grammar

不 (bù) is the most common negation word in Mandarin. It means "not" or "don't" and appears directly before verbs and adjectives to make them negative.

**Structure:** Subject + 不 + Verb/Adjective

Examples:
- 我不去。(Wǒ bù qù.) — I'm not going. / I don't go.
- 他不吃。(Tā bù chī.) — He's not eating. / He doesn't eat.
- 不好。(Bù hǎo.) — Not good.

不 negates present habits, future actions, and current states. It's your default negation word for most situations.

## [phase:atomic] Negating verbs with 不
tags: negation, bu, verbs

Use 不 before action verbs to express that you don't do something (habitually) or won't do something (in the future).

Examples:
- 我不喝茶。(Wǒ bù hē chá.) — I don't drink tea.
- 他不来。(Tā bù lái.) — He's not coming. / He won't come.
- 我们不看。(Wǒmen bù kàn.) — We're not watching. / We don't watch.

Remember: 不 works for present habits and future actions, but NOT for completed past actions (we'll use 没 for that).

## [phase:atomic] Negating adjectives with 不
tags: negation, bu, adjectives

不 also negates adjectives (descriptive words). This creates the opposite meaning or denies a quality.

Examples:
- 不贵。(Bù guì.) — Not expensive.
- 不大。(Bù dà.) — Not big.
- 不忙。(Bù máng.) — Not busy.
- 不冷。(Bù lěng.) — Not cold.

This is one of the simplest and most useful patterns in Mandarin. Just put 不 in front of any adjective to negate it.

## [phase:atomic] Introduction to 没 (méi)
tags: negation, mei, basic-grammar

没 (méi) is the second main negation word in Mandarin. While 不 is the default negator, 没 has specific uses:

1. To negate the verb 有 (yǒu, "to have")
2. To negate actions regardless of time — it indicates non-occurrence, not strictly past tense

没 is often written as 没有 (méiyǒu) in full form, meaning exactly the same thing. Both 没 and 没有 are correct and interchangeable.

Think of it this way: 不 is for general negation, while 没 is for "lacking" or "not having happened." 没 can express both simple past ("I didn't go") and present perfect contexts ("I haven't gone yet"), depending on context. The key distinction is that 没 indicates non-completion or non-occurrence, not strictly temporal direction.

## [phase:atomic] Negating 有 with 没
tags: negation, mei, you

The verb 有 (yǒu, "to have") is special — it can ONLY be negated with 没, never with 不.

**Wrong:** 我不有钱。❌
**Correct:** 我没有钱。(Wǒ méiyǒu qián.) ✓ — I don't have money.

More examples:
- 他没有书。(Tā méiyǒu shū.) — He doesn't have books.
- 我没有时间。(Wǒ méiyǒu shíjiān.) — I don't have time.
- 没有水。(Méiyǒu shuǐ.) — There's no water. / (I) don't have water.

Remember: 有 + 没 always; 有 + 不 never!

## [phase:atomic] 没 for completed actions with 了
tags: negation, mei, le, past-tense

When you want to say something DIDN'T happen (completed action negation), use 没. When 没 negates a completed action, you do NOT use 了.

**Affirmative:** 我吃了。(Wǒ chī le.) — I ate.
**Negative:** 我没吃。(Wǒ méi chī.) — I didn't eat.

More examples:
- 他没来。(Tā méi lái.) — He didn't come.
- 我没去。(Wǒ méi qù.) — I didn't go.
- 我们没看。(Wǒmen méi kàn.) — We didn't watch.

Notice: 没 + verb (no 了). The 了 disappears in the negative form.

## [phase:atomic] 不 vs 没: time reference
tags: negation, bu, mei, comparison

The key difference between 不 and 没 relates to completion and realization:

**不** = present habits, future actions, states, adjectives
- 我不去。(Wǒ bù qù.) — I don't go. / I won't go. / I'm not going.

**没** = actions that did not occur or are not yet completed (can include past, present perfect, or prospective contexts), and negating 有
- 我没去。(Wǒ méi qù.) — I didn't go. / I haven't gone.

Think of it this way:
- 不 describes what you generally don't do or won't do
- 没 indicates non-occurrence or non-completion — an action that didn't happen or hasn't been realized (可 for present perfect, past, or habitual negation depending on context)

## [phase:atomic] Common pattern: 不好
tags: negation, bu, bu-hao, common-patterns

不好 (bù hǎo) means "not good" and is extremely common in daily conversation. It's used to express that something is bad, wrong, or inappropriate.

Examples:
- 不好。(Bù hǎo.) — Not good. / That's bad.
- 天气不好。(Tiānqì bù hǎo.) — The weather is bad.
- 这个不好。(Zhège bù hǎo.) — This one is not good.

You can also use it to politely decline:
- 不好意思。(Bù hǎo yìsi.) — Sorry. / Excuse me. (literally "not good meaning")

## [phase:atomic] Common pattern: 没有
tags: negation, mei, meiyou, common-patterns

没有 (méiyǒu) as a standalone phrase has two main meanings:

1. **Don't have:** 我没有。(Wǒ méiyǒu.) — I don't have (any).
2. **Didn't / haven't:** Used alone as a response meaning "didn't" or "haven't."

Examples in context:
- A: 你有钱吗？(Nǐ yǒu qián ma?) — Do you have money?
- B: 没有。(Méiyǒu.) — No. / I don't have (any).

- A: 你吃了吗？(Nǐ chī le ma?) — Did you eat?
- B: 没有。(Méiyǒu.) — No. / I haven't.

没有 is one of the most useful two-syllable words in Mandarin for negative responses.

## [phase:complex] Choosing between 不 and 没 in context
tags: negation, bu, mei, comparison, context

When forming negative sentences, you must choose the correct negator based on what you're negating:

**Use 不 for:**
- Adjectives: 不好、不贵、不冷
- Verbs (habits/future): 不去、不吃、不学习
- States: 不是、不在

**Use 没 for:**
- The verb 有: 没有钱、没有时间
- Completed past actions: 没去、没吃、没看

**Quick test:** If you're talking about something that didn't happen (past) or you're negating 有, use 没. For everything else, use 不.

## [phase:complex] Negation with 是 (shì)
tags: negation, bu, shi, identity

The verb 是 (shì, "to be") follows the general rule: negate it with 不, not with 没.

**Structure:** Subject + 不是 + Noun/Identity

Examples:
- 我不是老师。(Wǒ bú shì lǎoshī.) — I'm not a teacher.
- 他不是学生。(Tā bú shì xuésheng.) — He's not a student.
- 这不是水。(Zhè bú shì shuǐ.) — This is not water.

Note: When 不 appears before 是, it's often pronounced "bú" (second tone) due to tone sandhi, but you can write it as 不是 in pinyin.

## [phase:complex] Double negation patterns
tags: negation, bu, mei, advanced-patterns

Sometimes you'll encounter constructions with both 不 and another negative element. Two common patterns:

**不是...吗？(Bú shì... ma?)** — Isn't it...? / Aren't you...?
- 你不是学生吗？(Nǐ bú shì xuésheng ma?) — Aren't you a student?

**没有 + negative meaning word**
- 没有问题。(Méiyǒu wèntí.) — No problem. (literally "don't have problem")

These aren't true double negatives (making a positive) like in English. They're fixed expressions where the negative words work together.

## [phase:complex] Negation in comparative contexts
tags: negation, bu, mei, comparison-sentences

When comparing things, 不 is used with comparison words:

**不太 (bú tài)** — not very / not too
- 不太好。(Bú tài hǎo.) — Not very good.
- 不太贵。(Bú tài guì.) — Not too expensive.

**不很 (bù hěn)** — not very (less common, as 很 is often neutral)
- 不很大。(Bù hěn dà.) — Not very big.

These soften negation, making statements less absolute. Instead of saying something is bad (不好), you can say it's "not very good" (不太好).

## [phase:complex] Sentence-final 了 vs negation
tags: negation, bu, mei, le, change-of-state

When 了 appears at the end of a sentence (indicating change of state or new situation), negation requires careful attention:

**Affirmative with 了:** 我忙了。(Wǒ máng le.) — I'm busy now. (change: wasn't before, am now)

**Negative:** You typically use 不 for current states:
- 我不忙了。(Wǒ bù máng le.) — I'm not busy anymore. (change: was busy, not now)

Or you might restructure:
- 我没那么忙。(Wǒ méi nàme máng.) — I'm not that busy.

The sentence-final 了 can appear with 不 to indicate a change to a negative state.

## [phase:integration] Negation in dialogue
tags: negation, bu, mei, dialogue, real-world

**Dialogue:**

A: 你今天去商店了吗？(Nǐ jīntiān qù shāngdiàn le ma?)
Did you go to the store today?

B: 没去。我没有时间。(Méi qù. Wǒ méiyǒu shíjiān.)
Didn't go. I don't have time.

A: 那明天去吗？(Nà míngtiān qù ma?)
Then will you go tomorrow?

B: 明天不去。太忙了。(Míngtiān bù qù. Tài máng le.)
Won't go tomorrow. Too busy.

A: 商店的东西贵吗？(Shāngdiàn de dōngxi guì ma?)
Are the store's things expensive?

B: 不太贵。(Bú tài guì.)
Not too expensive.

b: 我没去。(Wǒ méi qù.) — I didn't go.
c: 不太冷。(Bú tài lěng.) — Not too cold.
d: 我没看了电影。(Wǒ méi kàn le diànyǐng.) — I didn't watch a movie.
answer: ad
explanation: Option a uses 不有 instead of 没有 (有 must be negated with 没). Option d has the primary error of incorrect word order and form — should be 我没看电影 (没 negating completed action without 了). The 了 placement makes it doubly wrong, but the core issue is that 没 should not appear with 了 in this construction. Options b and c are correct.

### question ordering difficulty:3
tags: phase:integration, negation, bu, mei, sentence-building
Arrange these words to form: "I don't drink coffee, but I drank tea yesterday."
a: 我
b: 不喝咖啡
c: 但是
d: 昨天
e: 我喝了茶
answer: abcde
explanation: The correct order creates: 我不喝咖啡，但是昨天我喝了茶. Note: The second 我 can be omitted with the same subject, so 我不喝咖啡，但是昨天喝了茶 is also grammatically natural and acceptable.

### question singleChoice difficulty:4
tags: phase:integration, negation, bu, mei, context
You're at a restaurant. The waiter asks if you've ordered yet (点了吗？— Diǎn le ma?). You haven't. What do you reply?
a: 没有。(Méiyǒu.) or 还没。(Hái méi.)
b: 不。(Bù.)
c: 不点。(Bù diǎn.)
d: 没点了。(Méi diǎn le.)
answer: a
explanation: This is asking about a completed action (whether you've already ordered), so 没有 is correct. 还没 means "not yet" and is also appropriate.

### question freeText difficulty:4
tags: phase:integration, negation, bu, mei, comparison, synthesis
Explain when you would use 不好 versus 没有好 (if the latter even makes sense). What's the difference in meaning and usage?
answer: • 不好 (bù hǎo) means "not good" — negates the adjective 好, describes quality/state • 没有好 is not a standard Chinese expression • If you mean "don't have good (things)," you'd say 没有好的东西 • 不 negates adjectives directly; 没有 indicates lack of possession • Key point: 不好 is a quality judgment; you cannot use 没有 to negate adjectives directly
